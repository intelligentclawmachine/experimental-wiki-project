# The Psychology of Regret

**Source**: Research synthesis drawing on Gilovich & Medvec (1995), Kahneman & Tversky (1982), Camille et al. (2004), Schwartz (2004), Roese & Summerville (2005), Pink (2022), Ware, Zeelenberg & Pieters (2007), Neff.

## Key Takeaways

Regret is a cognitively sophisticated emotion requiring counterfactual reasoning — the ability to simulate "what might have been." It operates on two timescales: short-term action regrets that fade, and long-term inaction regrets that grow. Regret reveals values, and when processed well, serves as motivational data rather than ruminative suffering.

## Microscale Findings

- Action regrets dominate short-term; inaction regrets dominate long-term (Gilovich & Medvec)
- Orbitofrontal cortex is essential for regret — it requires comparison, not just disappointment (Camille et al.)
- People choose to minimize anticipated regret even against expected utility (regret aversion)
- Choice overload amplifies regret through more counterfactual comparisons (Schwartz)

## Lifetime Findings

- Education, career, and romance are the most common life regrets (Roese & Summerville)
- Pink's four core regrets: foundation, boldness (predominant), moral, connection
- Ware's dying regrets center on inauthenticity, overwork, and lost connections
- Healthy aging involves reduced regret sensitivity; failed regret regulation predicts late-life depression
