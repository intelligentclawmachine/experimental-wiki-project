# Boredom, Distress Intolerance, and Compulsive Stimulation

**Source**: Research synthesis drawing on Eastwood (2012), Westgate & Wilson (2018), Simons & Gaher (2005), Whitfield-Gabrieli & Ford (2012), Ward et al. (2017), Yildirim & Correia (2015).

## Key Takeaways

Boredom is an active aversive signal, not merely the absence of stimulation. When boredom is experienced as intolerable distress rather than a transient signal, it triggers compulsive stimulation seeking.

## Two Models of Boredom

- **Eastwood's attention model**: boredom arises from failure to direct attention toward satisfying activity
- **Westgate & Wilson's MAC model**: two independent pathways — attentional deficits (can't focus) and meaning deficits (activity lacks value). Either pathway produces boredom, even in stimulating environments.

## Distress Intolerance

Simons & Gaher's four dimensions: tolerance, appraisal, absorption, regulation. Low distress tolerance is transdiagnostic — predicts substance use, self-harm, binge eating, anxiety. Ellis's frustration intolerance: catastrophizing ordinary discomfort.

## The Default Mode Network Problem

During idle moments, the DMN surfaces self-referential thought, memories, fears. For people with trauma, anxiety, or ruminative tendencies, stillness becomes aversive because DMN activation brings painful internal content. DMN hyperactivity correlates with depression and rumination.

## Digital Age Amplification

The attention economy exploits boredom intolerance by design. Constant connectivity creates a feedback loop: immediate access to stimulation atrophies the ability to tolerate unstimulated states. Smartphone mere-presence reduces cognitive capacity.
