# Vagal Stimulation Model of Contemplative Activity

**Gerritsen & Band (2018)** explain why breathwork, meditation, yoga, and tai chi all reduce stress despite different philosophical origins: they all stimulate the vagus nerve through slow, controlled breathing.

## Two Stimulation Pathways

1. **Direct** — slow breathing activates vagal afferent fibers in the lungs, immediately lowering heart rate and blood pressure
2. **Indirect (biofeedback)** — resulting physiological calm signals safety to the CNS, reducing amygdala reactivity and strengthening prefrontal control

## Optimal Parameters

- **Rate:** ~6 breaths per minute (10-second cycle)
- **Ratio:** exhalation longer than inhalation
- **Technique:** diaphragmatic (belly) breathing
- **Reflexes engaged:** baroreceptor reflexes, Hering-Breuer reflex

## The Central Autonomic Network

The vagus nerve feeds into brain regions governing emotion and cognition:

- **Prefrontal cortex** — executive control
- **Anterior cingulate** — conflict monitoring, emotion regulation
- **Insula** — interoceptive awareness
- **Amygdala** — threat detection (dampened by vagal input)
- **Hippocampus** — memory and context

Enhanced vagal tone strengthens top-down control, weakens rumination patterns, and improves emotional regulation.

## Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

HRV is the primary biomarker of vagal tone:
- Higher HRV = better stress resilience, emotional regulation, cognitive flexibility
- Low HRV predicts cardiovascular disease, depression, anxiety
- Breathing practices measurably increase HRV

## The Unifying Mechanism

This resolves the puzzle of why diverse traditions produce similar outcomes — yoga, tai chi, meditation, qigong, and prayer all involve slow controlled breathing that stimulates the same vagal pathway. The common denominator is respiratory, not philosophical.
