# How and Why Humans Trust: Meta-Analysis

PMC (2023). 338 peer-reviewed studies (1974-2018), 2,185 effect sizes. The most comprehensive quantitative analysis of interpersonal [[trust|trust]] mechanisms.

## Three-Factor Model

### 1. Trustor Factors (who you are)
| Predictor | Effect (r) |
|-----------|-----------|
| Other trust measures | 0.47 |
| Commitment | 0.44 |
| Engagement | 0.40 |
| Self-efficacy | 0.36 |
| Gender | 0.10 (weak) |
| Prior experience | **0.00** (none!) |

### 2. Trustee Factors (who they are)
| Predictor | Effect (r) |
|-----------|-----------|
| Transparency | 0.48 |

| Team conflict | -0.25 |

## Headline Findings

**Reputation and closeness** are "the most predictive factors of trustworthiness outcome" in experimental studies.

**The Propensity Paradox**: Your general tendency to trust does NOT predict whether you actually trust specific people. Immediate experience overrides disposition — **"instant-trust"** sets a baseline that dominates subsequent interaction.

**Prior experience doesn't matter**: r = 0.00. What happened before has no correlation with current trust. Context and immediate impression dominate.

**Gender is irrelevant**: across all analyses, gender showed near-zero effects.

**In-group membership is enormous**: r = 0.57 — the single largest correlational effect. We trust people who are "like us."

**Inequality erodes trust**: "Those people who reside in areas with higher heterogeneity and inequality are, in general, less likely to trust."
