# Dunbar's Number and Relationship Layers

[[robin-dunbar|Robin Dunbar]]'s social brain hypothesis and the structure of human social networks.

## The Social Brain Hypothesis

Primates evolved large brains to manage complex social lives. Brain size correlates with group size across primate species. For humans, this yields a cognitive limit of ~150 stable relationships.

## The Layered Structure

Social networks organize into concentric circles, each **~3x** the size of the one inside it:

| Layer | Size | Frequency | What It Feels Like |
|-------|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Support clique** | ~5 | Weekly | "I'd call at 3am in a crisis" |
| **Sympathy group** | ~15 | Monthly | "I'd genuinely miss them" |
| **Active network** | ~50 | Periodically | "I'd stop and chat" |
| **Dunbar's number** | ~150 | Occasionally | "I know who they are and how they fit" |
| **Acquaintances** | ~500 | Rarely | "I recognize them and can place them" |
| **Recognizable faces** | ~1,500 | — | "I know their face" |

## Key Findings

- Neuroimaging (12+ studies): social network size correlates with default mode neural network size
- Relationship maintenance is **zero-sum for time** — more in one layer means less for others
- The layered structure appears universally in both personal networks and communities
- Individual variation exists in how people distribute investment across layers

## Implications for Meaningful Life

The ~5 support clique maps directly to the [[harvard-study-of-adult-development|Harvard Study]] finding that close relationship quality matters most. Quality over quantity is not just wisdom — it's a **cognitive constraint**. Humans literally cannot maintain unlimited deep connections.

Investing in the inner circles (5-15 people) yields the highest well-being returns. Maximizing outer circles (500+, social media followers) doesn't substitute.
