# Stimulation as Avoidance: Active Escape

**Source**: Research synthesis drawing on Hayes (ACT), Lembke (pleasure-pain balance), van der Kolk (trauma), Potenza (behavioral addictions), Sapolsky & Schultz (dopamine), Wells (metacognitive model), Nolen-Hoeksema (distraction coping).

## Key Takeaways

Experiential avoidance isn't limited to withdrawal and passivity. People commonly avoid internal states through *increased* activity — busyness, doom-scrolling, binge-watching, overworking. These behaviors share the same functional role as classic avoidance: preventing contact with distressing internal experience.

## Why Active Avoidance Is Harder to Detect

Active avoiders appear engaged, productive, and functional. The avoidance is hidden behind a surface of high performance or constant activity. This makes it clinically invisible and socially reinforced — workaholism is praised, not treated.

## Behavioral Addictions as Avoidance

Process addictions (gambling, gaming, shopping, social media) activate the same mesolimbic dopamine pathways as substance addictions. Negative reinforcement (distress relief) is often a stronger driver than positive reinforcement (pleasure) — the behavior persists not because it feels good, but because it prevents feeling bad.

## The Dopamine Trap

Dopamine encodes anticipation and novelty, not pleasure. Stimulation-seeking avoidance exploits this: novel stimuli trigger dopamine release that temporarily overrides negative affect. But tolerance develops — requiring escalating novelty. Lembke's pleasure-pain balance model: chronic overstimulation downregulates dopamine receptors, creating a baseline deficit that *increases* the original distress.
