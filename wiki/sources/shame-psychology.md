## Key Takeaways

- Lewis distinguished shame (global, "I am bad") from guilt (behavioral, "I did bad"); Tangney's research shows guilt-proneness is adaptive while shame-proneness correlates with anger and psychological symptoms
- Brown's shame resilience theory identifies empathy as the antidote to shame, which thrives in secrecy, silence, and judgment
- Nathanson's Compass of Shame maps four defensive responses: withdrawal, attack self, avoidance, and attack other — unacknowledged shame fuels humiliated fury
- Bradshaw distinguishes toxic shame (internalized defectiveness from chronic criticism/neglect) from healthy shame (signals social boundary violations)
- Shame is a strong predictor of depression and drives the use-remorse-relapse cycle in addiction

## Key Frameworks

- **Shame vs. Guilt** (Lewis, 1971; Tangney)
- **Shame Resilience Theory** (Brown)
- **Compass of Shame** (Nathanson, 1992)
- **Toxic vs. Healthy Shame** (Bradshaw, 1988)
