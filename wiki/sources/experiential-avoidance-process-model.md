# Experiential Avoidance: A Process Model

**Wang, Tian, & Yang (2024)** propose an integrated model of how experiential avoidance — the unwillingness to remain in contact with uncomfortable internal experiences — develops and self-reinforces.

## Five-Stage Cascade

The model traces avoidance through a predictable sequence:

1. **Emotional trigger** — a situation activates negative affect
2. **Strategy selection** — suppression/avoidance is chosen over approach
3. **Short-term relief** — distress temporarily drops (negative reinforcement)
4. **Habitual consolidation** — relief reinforces avoidance as the go-to strategy
5. **Maladaptive entrenchment** — avoidance becomes automatic, narrowing the behavioral repertoire

The key insight is that avoidance *works* in the moment — that's precisely why it's so persistent.

## The Downward Spiral

Paradoxically, avoidance worsens the very experiences it aims to escape:

- **Attentional bias** — hypervigilance increases as avoidance prevents habituation
- **Working memory drain** — suppression consumes cognitive resources needed for daily functioning
- **Memory rebound** — suppressed memories paradoxically intrude more forcefully (Wegner's ironic process theory)
- **Emotional amplification** — avoided emotions return at greater intensity

## Language and Avoidance

Drawing on Hayes' Relational Frame Theory, the paper explains why humans are uniquely susceptible: language creates bidirectional associations, so merely *thinking* about a feared experience triggers the same emotional response as the experience itself. This makes even internal events targets for avoidance — a trap other species don't face.

## Clinical Connections

Experiential avoidance is a transdiagnostic factor linked to:

- **Depression** — rumination as a form of cognitive avoidance
- **Anxiety** — behavioral avoidance maintaining fear cycles
- **OCD** — thought suppression paradox (trying not to think increases intrusions)
- **PTSD** — emotional numbing and avoidance as core symptom clusters
