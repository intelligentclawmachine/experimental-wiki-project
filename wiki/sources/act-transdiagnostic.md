# Acceptance and Commitment Therapy: A Transdiagnostic Intervention

**Dindo, Van Liew, & Arch (2017)** present ACT as the primary clinical response to experiential avoidance, built around cultivating psychological flexibility.

## Psychological Flexibility

Defined as "the ability to persist in or change behavior when it is in service of valued ends." This is the inverse of experiential avoidance:

- **Avoidance** = narrowing behavior to escape discomfort
- **Flexibility** = expanding behavior in service of values *despite* discomfort

## The Hexaflex: Six Core Processes

ACT organizes its interventions around six interconnected processes:

1. **Present moment awareness** — contacting the here-and-now rather than ruminating or worrying
2. **Cognitive defusion** — observing thoughts as mental events, not literal truths ("I notice I'm having the thought that...")
3. **Acceptance** — willingness to experience difficult feelings without trying to eliminate them
4. **Self-as-context** — a stable observing self that isn't defined by any particular thought or feeling
5. **Values clarification** — identifying what truly matters as a compass for behavior
6. **Committed action** — concrete steps aligned with values, even when uncomfortable

## Evidence Base

As of 2016: 136 published RCTs with medium effect sizes vs. treatment-as-usual and large effects vs. wait-list controls. The APA recognizes ACT as empirically supported for depression, mixed anxiety, psychosis, chronic pain, and OCD.

## Transdiagnostic Reach

Because ACT targets the *process* (avoidance) rather than specific diagnoses, it applies across mental health, chronic medical conditions, behavioral health, and performance contexts.

## ACT vs. CBT

A key distinction: CBT focuses on changing the *content* of thoughts (cognitive restructuring), while ACT focuses on changing the *relationship* to thoughts (defusion). Both have strong evidence and work through different mechanisms.
