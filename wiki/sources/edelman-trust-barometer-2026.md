# 2026 Edelman Trust Barometer

**Edelman (2026)** presents the 26th annual global survey of institutional trust, revealing a shift from grievance to insularity.

## The Insularity Shift

The dominant finding: **70% of respondents** are unwilling or hesitant to trust someone with different values, information sources, or cultural background. Trust is becoming tribal — high within in-groups, low toward out-groups.

This maps directly to the meta-analysis finding that in-group membership has the largest effect on trust (r = 0.57). (Source: [Meta-Analysis](../sources/meta-analysis-how-humans-trust.md))

## Key Data Points

- **32% globally** believe the next generation will be better off
- The income-based trust gap has **more than doubled since 2012** (29-point gap in U.S.)
- Trust in government leaders dropped **16 points** over five years
- **Business** is the only institution seen as both ethical and competent

## Institutional Trust Hierarchy

Institutions that deliver tangible value retain trust; those perceived as self-serving lose it. This echoes O'Neill's philosophical argument: the goal should be making institutions *trustworthy*, not manufacturing *trust*.

## Connections to Individual Psychology

- **Insularity** mirrors Dunbar's layers — under threat, people shrink to inner circles
- **Inequality eroding trust** confirms the meta-analysis contextual factor
- **Institutional distrust** may drive compensatory reliance on personal [[relationships]] — consistent with the Harvard Study finding
- The retreat into ideological circles reflects [[experiential-avoidance|avoidance]] at a societal level — avoiding the discomfort of engaging with different perspectives
