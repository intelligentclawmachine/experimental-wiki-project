# Philosophy of Trust

**Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (2025)** and the **Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy** provide the authoritative survey of philosophical perspectives on trust.

## Trust vs. Mere Reliance

The foundational question: you rely on your alarm clock, but you don't *trust* it. What's the difference?

- Trust involves vulnerability to **betrayal**, not just **disappointment**
- When trust is violated, we feel a specifically moral emotion — betrayal — that doesn't apply to unreliable objects
- Annette Baier: trust requires the trustee's **goodwill** toward the truster

This distinction sharpens the psychological research: [[trust]] isn't just prediction about behavior — it's a moral relationship involving commitment and vulnerability to a specific kind of harm.

## Major Theories

| Theory | Core Claim | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Baier (Goodwill)** | Trust requires believing the other acts from benevolence | May be too demanding — we trust professionals without requiring personal care |
| **Hardin (Encapsulated Interests)** | Trust = believing the other has incorporated your interests into theirs | Grounded in rational self-interest and reputation |
| **Holton (Participant Stance)** | Trust = treating someone as a responsible, accountable agent | Trust is an attitude, not just a belief |
| **Jones (Trust-Responsiveness)** | We trust people motivated by the fact we're counting on them | Trust itself creates a reason for trustworthiness |
| **Hawley (Commitment)** | Trust = relying on someone to fulfill their commitments | Explains why broken promises feel like betrayal but incompetence doesn't |
| **O'Neill (Trustworthiness)** | Don't aim to increase trust — aim to place trust well | The goal is better judgment, not more trust |

## Therapeutic Trust

A philosophically important concept: trusting someone *in order to make them trustworthy*. Parents do this with children; therapists with clients; partners in [[attachment-theory|earned security]] journeys. Trust here is an investment in creating trustworthiness, not a response to evidence of it.

## Trust and Testimonial Justice

Fricker's concept: **testimonial injustice** occurs when people are systematically distrusted based on prejudice. This connects trust to social justice — distrust of certain groups is both epistemically wrong and morally harmful.

## O'Neill's Provocation

Onora O'Neill argues that modern accountability regimes (audits, targets, transparency mandates) can paradoxically *undermine* trust. The proper goal isn't more trust — it's making institutions more *trustworthy* and helping people *place trust well*.
