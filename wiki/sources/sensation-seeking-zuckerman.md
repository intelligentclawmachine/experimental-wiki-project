# Sensation Seeking: Biosocial Bases

**Source**: Marvin Zuckerman, *Behavioral Expressions and Biosocial Bases of Sensation Seeking* (1994), plus related research (SSS-V, 1978; Roberti 2004; Hittner & Swickert 2006).

## Key Takeaways

Sensation seeking is a personality trait defined as the pursuit of varied, novel, complex, and intense experiences — and willingness to take risks for them. Zuckerman's Sensation Seeking Scale (Form V) measures four dimensions:

1. **Thrill and Adventure Seeking** — desire for physical danger, speed, gravity
2. **Experience Seeking** — novel sensory/mental experiences, unconventional lifestyle
3. **Disinhibition** — social stimulation, parties, sexual variety, alcohol
4. **Boredom Susceptibility** — aversion to repetition, restlessness without stimulation

## Biological Basis

- High sensation seekers have **lower baseline cortical arousal** or a higher optimal set-point (building on Hebb's optimal arousal theory)
- **Low MAO-B activity** correlates with high sensation seeking — more available dopamine
- **DRD4 polymorphisms** linked to individual differences in sensation seeking
- High seekers show **augmenting cortical responses** to increasing stimulus intensity

## Risk and Addiction

One of the strongest personality predictors of substance use initiation in adolescence. Disinhibition and Boredom Susceptibility subscales most associated with alcohol/drug use. Sensation seeking is correlated with but distinct from impulsivity (Whiteside & Lynam UPPS model).

## Critical Distinction

The same sensation-seeking behavior can serve fundamentally different psychological functions:
- **Approach-motivated**: genuine pursuit of novelty and reward
- **Avoidance-motivated**: using stimulation to flee discomfort (bridges to Khantzian's self-medication hypothesis and Hayes's experiential avoidance framework)

## Relevance

Provides the personality framework for understanding why some people are biologically predisposed to seek intense stimulation — and when that seeking crosses from healthy exploration to [avoidance-driven compulsion](../concepts/stimulation-based-avoidance.md).
