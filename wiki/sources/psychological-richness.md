# Psychological Richness: A Third Path to the Good Life

Research by [[erin-westgate|Erin Westgate]] (University of Florida) and [[shigehiro-oishi|Shigehiro Oishi]] (University of Chicago). Term introduced in 2022, building on 2015 scale development. Published in *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* (2025).

## The Three Dimensions of Well-Being

Traditional well-being research focused on two paths:

1. **Hedonic (Happiness)** — positive emotions, pleasure, comfort, life satisfaction
2. **Eudaimonic (Meaning)** — purpose, significance, coherence

Westgate and Oishi propose a third:

3. **[[psychological-richness|Psychological Richness]]** — diverse, interesting, perspective-changing experiences

## What Makes a Life Psychologically Rich?

- Experiences that challenge, change perspective, and satisfy curiosity
- Both external (travel, new challenges) and internal (literature, music, deep conversation)
- Need not be pleasant or purposeful — a hurricane can be "rich"
- "A psychologically rich life can come from something as simple as reading a great novel or hearing a haunting song"

## Key Findings

- The concept resonates across cultures
- Those pursuing service to others had the strongest sense of psychological richness
- Trade-offs exist: "Interesting experiences aren't always pleasant experiences"
- People ideally want all three dimensions, but may prioritize differently

## Significance

This reframes the question "what is a good life?" from a binary (happy vs. meaningful) to a three-dimensional space. Some people don't primarily seek happiness or purpose — they seek depth, variety, and growth through experience.
