# Psychological Safety in Teams

**Amy Edmondson (1999, Administrative Science Quarterly)** introduces "team psychological safety" — a shared belief that a team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking — and shows its link to learning behavior across 51 work teams.

## Definition

"A shared belief held by members of a team that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking." Team members believe they can ask questions, admit mistakes, propose ideas, and challenge the status quo without punishment.

## Safety vs. Trust

A critical distinction that sharpens both concepts:

| | Psychological Safety | Trust |
|---|---|---|
| **Level** | Group climate | Dyadic relationship |
| **Focus** | Can I take risks *here*? | Can I rely on *you*? |
| **Scope** | "How things work in this team" | "What I believe about this person" |
| **Mechanism** | Shared norms reduce fear | Accumulated experience builds confidence |

Psychological safety is the *soil*; [[trust]] is the *plant* that grows in it. Safe teams create the conditions for trust to develop through repeated positive interactions.

## The Error-Reporting Paradox

Edmondson's landmark finding: the best hospital teams reported **more errors**, not fewer. Because they felt safe reporting, they learned faster and performed better. Teams with low safety hid errors, creating the illusion of competence.

This extends to any learning context: psychological safety predicts not fewer mistakes but faster learning from them.

## How Leaders Build Safety

1. **Frame work as learning** — not as execution to be judged
2. **Acknowledge fallibility** — "I may miss something, I need to hear from you"
3. **Model curiosity** — ask questions rather than give answers
4. **Respond productively to failure** — focus on learning, not blame
