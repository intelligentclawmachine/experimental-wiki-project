# Resilience

The capacity to maintain or regain healthy functioning in the face of adversity. Not a rare trait of exceptional individuals but what Ann Masten calls **"ordinary magic"** — a common human capacity that arises from basic adaptational systems.

## What Resilience Is (And Isn't)

Two important distinctions (Source: [Resilience Psychology](../sources/resilience-psychology.md)):

**Bounce-back vs. bounce-forward**: resilience is sometimes returning to baseline after adversity (bounce-back) and sometimes reaching a new, higher level of functioning (bounce-forward). The latter overlaps with [post-traumatic growth](../concepts/post-traumatic-growth.md) but is not identical — growth involves qualitative transformation of identity and worldview.

**Resilience vs. invulnerability**: resilient people still suffer. They feel pain, grief, fear. The difference is that these experiences don't permanently derail functioning. Resilience is not the absence of distress but the capacity to function *through* it.

## Four Trajectories After Adversity

George Bonanno's research identified four distinct post-adversity patterns:

| Trajectory | Pattern | Prevalence |
|---|---|---|
| **Resilience** | Stable healthy functioning throughout | Most common |
| **Recovery** | Initial distress → gradual return to baseline | Common |
| **Delayed** | Initial stability → later-emerging difficulties | Less common |
| **Chronic** | Persistent dysfunction | Least common |

The key finding: **resilience is the most common response** to loss and trauma, challenging the clinical assumption that distress is universal after adversity. Most people cope better than therapists expect.

## What Builds Resilience

The Kauai Longitudinal Study (Werner & Smith): among ~700 children exposed to significant adversity, one-third thrived. Three protective factors consistently emerged:

1. **At least one stable, caring relationship** — the single most powerful protective factor
2. **Internal locus of control** — believing you can influence outcomes
3. **Self-regulatory capacity** — managing emotions and impulses

The converging evidence across decades of research points to three pillars:
- **[Social support](../concepts/relationships.md)** — the most reliable buffer
- **Cognitive flexibility** — the ability to reappraise situations (see [psychological flexibility](../concepts/psychological-flexibility.md))
- **Physical health** — [sleep](../concepts/sleep.md), exercise, and [vagal tone](../concepts/vagal-tone-stress-regulation.md)

## Psychological Capital (PsyCap)

Fred Luthans developed PsyCap as a composite of four evidence-based capacities:

| Component | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Self-efficacy** | Belief in capability |
| **Optimism** | Positive attribution style |
| **[Hope](../concepts/hope.md)** | Agency + pathways |
| **Resilience** | Bounce-back from adversity |

PsyCap predicts workplace performance, job satisfaction, and well-being — and all four components are developable through targeted interventions.

## Grit

Angela Duckworth's construct: **passion + perseverance for long-term goals**. Grit overlaps with resilience but emphasizes sustained effort over time rather than response to acute adversity. Grit predicts graduation rates, job retention, and achievement beyond what talent or IQ predict. The connection to [motivation](../concepts/motivation.md) is direct: grit is sustained approach motivation despite obstacles.
