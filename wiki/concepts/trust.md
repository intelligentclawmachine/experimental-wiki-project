# Trust

"Choosing to make something important to you vulnerable to the actions of someone else." ([[brene-brown|Brené Brown]], via Charles Feltman)

Trust is the mechanism through which [[relationships|relationships]] deepen, the foundation on which [[vulnerability|vulnerability]] rests, and a major predictor of life satisfaction across 2.5 million participants.

---

## Two Dimensions: Interpersonal and Intrapersonal

### Interpersonal Trust (Trust in Others)

The willingness to accept vulnerability based on positive expectations of another's behavior. This is the trust between people — in couples, friendships, teams, communities.

**The evidence is massive**: a *Psychological Bulletin* meta-analysis (2025) of 500+ studies and 2.5 million participants found people who trust others more are happier and more satisfied. (Source: [GG 2025](../sources/greater-good-insights-2025.md))

### Intrapersonal Trust ([[self-trust|Self-Trust]])

"The firm reliance on the integrity of yourself" — trusting yourself to be kind to yourself regardless of outcomes. (Source: [Self-Trust](../sources/self-trust-psychology.md))

Brown's key insight: **you cannot trust others more than you trust yourself.** Self-trust is the foundation, not a bonus. See [[self-trust|Self-Trust]] for the full concept page.

## What Predicts Trust? (338 Studies, 2,185 Effect Sizes)

The most comprehensive meta-analysis of trust (Source: [Meta-Analysis](../sources/meta-analysis-how-humans-trust.md)) found three factor categories:

### The Trustor (You)
What about *you* predicts whether you'll trust?
- **Commitment** to the relationship: r = 0.44
- **Engagement**: r = 0.40
- **Self-efficacy** (self-trust): r = 0.36
- Gender, age, education: near-zero effects
- **Prior experience: r = 0.00** — your past doesn't predict your present trust

### The Trustee (Them)
What about *them* earns trust?
- **Transparency**: r = 0.48 (strongest)
- **Expertise**: r = 0.41
- **Reliability**: r = 0.32
- **Reputation**: r = 0.27
- Physical appearance, personality traits: near-zero effects

### The Context (Environment)
What about the *situation* enables trust?
- **In-group membership**: r = 0.57 (**largest effect in the entire meta-analysis**)
- **Shared mental models**: r = 0.42
- **Team cohesion**: r = 0.37
- **Communication**: r = 0.34
- **Team conflict**: r = -0.25 (destroys trust)
- **Inequality**: people in unequal areas trust less

### Surprising Findings

- **Instant trust dominates**: your general propensity to trust does NOT predict whether you actually trust specific people. Immediate experience overrides disposition.
- **Reputation + closeness** are the two most predictive experimental factors overall
- **Gender is irrelevant** to trust across all analyses
- **Prior experience doesn't matter** (r = 0.00) — each relationship gets its own trust baseline

## How Trust Builds (Gottman)

[[john-gottman|Gottman]]'s 40+ years of research shows trust builds through **everyday micro-moments**, not grand gestures. (Source: [Gottman](../sources/gottman-trust-and-relationships.md))

### Bids for Connection
People make small bids constantly — sharing a thought, pointing something out, asking a question. Partners can:
- **Turn toward** (engage) → trust deposits
- **Turn away** (ignore) → trust withdrawals
- **Turn against** (criticize) → trust destruction

Masters of relationships turn toward 86% of the time.

### The Trust/Betrayal Metric
When your partner's gain feels like your loss (zero-sum), that's betrayal. When you act to increase each other's wellbeing despite disagreement (cooperative-gain), that's trust.

**Life-or-death finding**: 58% of zero-sum husbands died in 20 years vs. 20% of cooperative-gain husbands.

### The ATTUNE Framework
**A**wareness → **T**urning toward → **T**olerance → **U**nderstanding → **N**on-defensive → **E**mpathy

## How Trust Builds (Brown)

### The BRAVING Framework
Seven elements, applicable to both interpersonal and self-trust:

- **B**oundaries — respect limits
- **R**eliability — do what you say
- **A**ccountability — own mistakes
- **V**ault — keep confidences
- **I**ntegrity — practice values, don't just profess them
- **N**on-judgment — let people struggle without judgment
- **G**enerosity — assume best intentions

### The Marble Jar
Trust fills like a marble jar — one small moment at a time. It empties the same way. Consistent small actions matter more than occasional grand ones.

## How Trust Breaks

### Gottman's Four Horsemen
1. **Contempt** — the strongest single predictor of relationship dissolution
2. **Criticism** — attacking character
3. **Defensiveness** — refusing accountability
4. **Stonewalling** — withdrawing

### The Betrayal Cascade
Zero-sum thinking → reduced commitment → resentment → diminished investment → escalated conflict → dissolution

### Structural Trust Erosion
The meta-analysis shows inequality and heterogeneity erode baseline trust at a community level. This is systemic, not personal.

## The Trust-Vulnerability Loop

Trust and [[vulnerability|vulnerability]] are reciprocal:
1. Trust enables vulnerability (feeling safe → taking emotional risks)
2. Vulnerability builds trust (being seen → connection → trust)
3. The loop compounds or collapses

This loop is the engine of relationship deepening. It explains why relationships either grow closer over time or drift apart — there's no neutral gear.

## The Neuroscience of Trust

[[paul-zak|Paul Zak's]] research reveals trust has a neurochemical substrate: oxytocin. (Source: [Neuroscience of Trust](../sources/neuroscience-of-trust.md))

- When someone trusts you, your brain releases oxytocin
- Oxytocin motivates you to reciprocate trust
- Reciprocation triggers oxytocin in the original truster
- A neurochemical feedback loop forms — trust compounds biologically

**Stress inhibits oxytocin** — this is why high-stress environments suppress trust, and why [[allostatic-load|allostatic load]] damages not just health but the capacity for connection.

## What Trust Is (Philosophy)

The philosophical literature sharpens what we mean by "trust" (Source: [Philosophy of Trust](../sources/philosophy-of-trust.md)):

- Trust is not mere reliance — you rely on an alarm clock, but you don't *trust* it
- The difference: trust involves vulnerability to **betrayal**, not just disappointment
- Multiple competing theories exist, but all agree trust involves a moral relationship, not just prediction
- O'Neill's provocation: don't aim for more trust — aim to *place trust well* and *refuse trust well*

## Trust Repair

When trust breaks, the repair path depends on the type of violation (Source: [Trust Repair Review](../sources/trust-repair-review.md)):

- **Competence violations** (inability) → relatively repairable through demonstrated improvement
- **Integrity violations** (dishonesty) → much harder; trust damaged by deception **never fully recovers**
- Five repair strategies: proactive transparency, active monitoring, remorse, shared activities, communication of reasons

See [[trust-repair|Trust Repair]] for the full concept page.

## Trust Beyond Relationships

### Psychological Safety (Teams)
[[amy-edmondson|Edmondson's]] research shows trust also operates at the group level through [[psychological-safety|psychological safety]] — a shared belief that a team is safe for risk-taking. Safety is the soil; trust is the plant. (Source: [Edmondson](../sources/psychological-safety-edmondson.md))

### Institutional Trust (Society)
The 2026 Edelman Trust Barometer shows trust becoming increasingly tribal: 70% of people are unwilling to trust those with different values. Business is the only institution seen as both ethical and competent. The income-based trust gap has more than doubled since 2012. (Source: [Edelman](../sources/edelman-trust-barometer-2026.md))
