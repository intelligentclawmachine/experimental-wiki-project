# Attachment Theory

A framework explaining how early bonding experiences shape relationship patterns across the lifespan. Originated by [[john-bowlby|John Bowlby]], extended to adult relationships by [[cindy-hazan|Cindy Hazan]] and Phillip Shaver (1987).

## The Attachment System

A continuous internal monitor asking: **"Is my attachment figure nearby, accessible, and attentive?"**

- **YES** → security, confidence, capacity for exploration and connection
- **NO** → anxiety, searching, clinging, protest, or withdrawal

This system doesn't shut off in adulthood — it transfers to romantic partners, close friends, and sometimes institutions or communities.

## The Four Adult Styles

| | Low Avoidance | High Avoidance |
|---|---|---|
| **Low Anxiety** | **Secure**: comfortable with intimacy and independence | **Dismissive**: self-reliant, uncomfortable with dependence |
| **High Anxiety** | **Preoccupied**: craves closeness, fears rejection | **Fearful**: wants closeness but fears it |

~60% of people show secure attachment patterns. Attachment is **dimensional** (a spectrum), not categorical (a fixed type).

## Key Research Findings

- Secure adults: greater satisfaction, longevity, [[trust|trust]], commitment, interdependence
- Secure adults both **seek AND provide** support effectively
- Partner preference mirrors infant patterns: warmth, responsiveness, attentiveness
- Stability from infancy to adulthood: r = .17 (significant but modest — attachment **changes**)

## Earned Security

**Critical finding**: attachment security can be developed in adulthood through consistent positive relationship experiences. This means:
- A person with anxious attachment can become more secure through a relationship with a responsive partner
- Therapy can build "earned security" — working through past attachment wounds
- The [[harvard-study-of-adult-development|Harvard Study]] found that even those with difficult beginnings could flourish

This connects to the broader theme: **it's never too late**. Relationships are not deterministic.

## How Attachment Shapes Meaning

Attachment style influences:
- Capacity for [[vulnerability|vulnerability]] (secure = easier; avoidant = harder)
- Ability to build and maintain [[trust|trust]]
- Response to conflict (anxious = escalate; avoidant = withdraw; secure = engage)
- Capacity for [[forgiveness|forgiveness]] (secure = more forgiving)
- Use of [[mature-coping-mechanisms|mature coping]] vs. immature defenses
