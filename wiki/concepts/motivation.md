# Motivation

The processes that give behavior its energy and direction. Understanding motivation means understanding why people act, persist, give up, or never start — and why the same person can be driven in one domain and paralyzed in another.

## The SDT Continuum

[Self-Determination Theory](../concepts/self-determination-theory.md) (Deci & Ryan) provides the most comprehensive framework, describing motivation as a continuum from controlled to autonomous:

Amotivation → External → Introjected → Identified → Integrated → Intrinsic

Movement toward internalization — making external goals feel like "mine" — is the process through which motivation becomes sustainable and well-being-promoting. (Source: [Motivation Psychology](../sources/motivation-psychology.md))

## Goal-Setting Theory

Locke & Latham's decades of research established that **specific, challenging goals** produce higher performance than vague or easy goals. The mechanism: goals direct attention, energize effort, and encourage persistence. But goals require adequate feedback and commitment — without these, specific goals can actually increase anxiety.

## Expectancy-Value Theory

Eccles & Wigfield: motivation is a function of **expectancy of success** ("Can I do this?") multiplied by **subjective task value** ("Do I want to?"). Value has four components:
- **Intrinsic value** — enjoyment
- **Utility value** — usefulness for other goals
- **Attainment value** — importance to identity
- **Cost** — what you give up

When either expectancy or value is zero, motivation is zero — regardless of how high the other is.

## Approach vs. Avoidance Motivation

Andrew Elliot's framework distinguishes two fundamentally different motivational systems:

| System | Direction | Driver | Neurobiological Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Approach** | Toward positive outcomes | Desire, curiosity, reward | BAS (dopamine) |
| **Avoidance** | Away from negative outcomes | Fear, anxiety, threat | BIS (serotonin, norepinephrine) |

The same goal can be approach-framed ("I want to succeed") or avoidance-framed ("I don't want to fail"). Avoidance motivation predicts greater anxiety, poorer performance under pressure, and less enjoyment — even when outcomes are identical.

This distinction echoes throughout the wiki: [sensation seeking](../concepts/sensation-seeking.md) can be approach or avoidance-motivated; [meaningful work](../concepts/meaningful-work.md) driven by calling vs. driven by fear of failure; [stimulation-based avoidance](../concepts/stimulation-based-avoidance.md) as the avoidance system hijacking behavior.

## Procrastination: Emotion Regulation Failure

Timothy Pychyl and Fuschia Sirois reframed procrastination — not as a time management problem but as an **[emotion regulation](../concepts/emotional-regulation.md) failure**. People avoid tasks to escape task-related negative affect (boredom, anxiety, frustration), prioritizing short-term mood repair over long-term goals. This is [experiential avoidance](../concepts/experiential-avoidance.md) applied to tasks.

The irony: procrastination produces guilt and shame, which create more negative affect, which drives more avoidance. The loop mirrors the [stimulation-avoidance paradox](../synthesis/stimulation-avoidance-paradox.md).

## Self-Efficacy

Albert Bandura: **belief in your capability to execute specific behaviors** is one of the strongest predictors of performance across domains. Self-efficacy is not global self-esteem — it's domain-specific confidence built through:
- Mastery experiences (most powerful)
- Vicarious learning (seeing others succeed)
- Verbal persuasion (encouragement)
- Physiological states (interpreting arousal as readiness vs. anxiety)

Self-efficacy mediates the relationship between ability and action — talent without belief in that talent produces inaction.
