# Nature and Well-being

Contact with nature — especially biodiverse natural environments — measurably improves mental health and is a significant source of meaning across cultures.

## Biodiversity Effect (2024)

Ryan Hammoud (King's College London), Urban Mind App study tracking ~2,000 people over two weeks. (Source: [GG 2024](../sources/greater-good-insights-2024.md))

- Observing natural elements (trees, plants, birds, water) → greater mental well-being
- Effects last up to **8 hours**
- Each additional natural element → **0.91-point** mental health increase on a 50-point scale
- **Biodiversity matters, not just "green"** — polycultural spaces outperform monoculture parks

Recommendation: "Moving away from monocultural pockets...towards polycultural spaces which mimic natural ecosystems."

## Nature as Meaning Source (Pew 2021)

Nature/outdoors is a notable source of meaning in the UK, Australia, France, New Zealand, and Sweden. Left-leaning individuals cite nature more often. (Source: [Pew Survey](../sources/pew-what-makes-life-meaningful.md))

## Indigenous Perspective

Small-scale societies living in close contact with nature report 6.8/10 life satisfaction on <$1,000/year income — higher than many wealthy-nation respondents. Nature proximity is one of the key non-economic factors. (Source: [GG 2024](../sources/greater-good-insights-2024.md))
