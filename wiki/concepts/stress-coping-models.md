# Stress Coping Models

How people respond to stress determines not just how they feel but how their bodies age, their relationships develop, and their sense of meaning evolves. Coping is the bridge between a stressful event and its long-term impact.

## The Transactional Model (Lazarus & Folkman)

The foundational insight: stress is not a property of events — it arises from the *relationship* between person and situation.

1. **Primary appraisal** — "Is this a threat, a harm, or a challenge?"
2. **Secondary appraisal** — "What can I do? Do I have the resources?"
3. **Coping response** — selected based on both appraisals

The same event (job loss, diagnosis, breakup) can be appraised as devastating or as an opportunity depending on perceived resources. Changing the appraisal changes the stress response — this is the mechanism behind cognitive reframing. (Source: [Coping Taxonomy](../sources/coping-mechanisms-taxonomy.md))

## Four Categories of Coping

### Problem-Focused
Change the situation directly: active coping, planning, seeking practical help. Most effective when the stressor is controllable.

### Emotion-Focused
Manage the emotional response: positive reframing, acceptance, humor, spirituality, emotional support. Most effective when the stressor is uncontrollable.

### Meaning-Focused
Derive significance from adversity: values-based reinterpretation, finding purpose in suffering. Related to Frankl's logotherapy and [post-traumatic growth](../concepts/post-traumatic-growth.md).

### Social Coping
Leverage relationships: support-seeking, communal coping, social comparison. Effective across contexts and directly buffers [allostatic load](../concepts/allostatic-load.md).

## Proactive vs. Reactive Coping

- **Reactive** — responding after stressors hit
- **Proactive** — anticipating stressors and building resources in advance

Proactive coping maps directly to Vaillant's mature defense of **anticipation** (Source: [Mature Coping](../concepts/mature-coping-mechanisms.md)) and consistently predicts better outcomes.

## Maladaptive Coping

The common thread in maladaptive coping is [experiential avoidance](../concepts/experiential-avoidance.md):

- **Behavioral disengagement** — giving up, withdrawing
- **Mental disengagement** — distraction, denial, rumination
- **Substance use** — chemical avoidance of emotional pain
- **Venting without action** — emotional discharge without problem-solving
- **Self-blame** — internalizing without constructive reflection

## The Body Keeps Score

Coping strategies don't just affect mood — they shape biology. Chronic maladaptive coping drives [allostatic load](../concepts/allostatic-load.md) through sustained HPA axis activation, while adaptive coping (social support, exercise, mindfulness) actively reduces physiological wear and tear.

## Integration with Defense Mechanisms

Coping strategies and [defense mechanisms](../concepts/defense-mechanisms.md) describe overlapping phenomena from different angles:

- **Defenses** are largely unconscious, automatic, and described in psychodynamic terms
- **Coping strategies** are often conscious, deliberate, and described in cognitive-behavioral terms
- Vaillant's mature defenses (humor, sublimation, anticipation) align with adaptive coping
- Immature defenses (denial, projection, acting out) align with maladaptive avoidant coping
