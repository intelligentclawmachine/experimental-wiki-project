# Grief and Loss

Grief is the natural human response to loss — not a disorder to be treated but a process to be supported. Modern grief research has moved far beyond stage models to embrace the complexity, individuality, and often non-linear nature of bereavement.

## Beyond the Five Stages

Elisabeth Kübler-Ross's five stages (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance) opened public discourse about death but have been largely superseded in academic psychology. The model was based on dying patients, not bereaved survivors, and lacked empirical validation. Grief is not a linear progression through discrete phases. (Source: [Grief and Loss](../sources/grief-and-loss.md))

## The Dual Process Model

Stroebe & Schut (1999) offer the most empirically supported framework. Adaptive grieving involves **oscillation** between two orientations:

- **Loss-oriented coping** — processing grief pain, yearning for the deceased, confronting the reality of loss
- **Restoration-oriented coping** — attending to life changes, learning new roles, rebuilding routine, taking breaks from grief

**Healthy grievers move between these modes.** Getting stuck in either is problematic: all loss-orientation produces overwhelming distress; all restoration-orientation produces delayed grief that surfaces later.

## Continuing Bonds

Klass, Silverman, & Nickman challenged the older assumption that healthy grief requires "breaking bonds" with the deceased. Maintaining connection — through memory, conversation, ritual, or symbolic representation — is **normative and often healthy**. The question is not whether you maintain the bond, but how: flexible, comforting bonds support adaptation; rigid, demanding bonds impede it.

## Worden's Four Tasks of Mourning

1. **Accept the reality** of the loss
2. **Process the pain** of grief
3. **Adjust to a world** without the deceased
4. **Find an enduring connection** with the deceased while embarking on a new life

These are tasks, not stages — they can be worked on simultaneously and revisited.

## When Grief Becomes Disordered

**Prolonged grief disorder** (DSM-5-TR, ICD-11) occurs when grief remains persistently debilitating beyond 12 months. Prigerson & Shear identified hallmarks:
- Intense yearning that doesn't diminish
- Identity disruption ("Part of me died with them")
- Emotional numbness and disbelief
- Difficulty re-engaging with life

This affects roughly 7-10% of bereaved individuals. Treatment (Shear's complicated grief therapy) involves gradual exposure to loss reminders and restoration-oriented engagement.

## Disenfranchised Grief

Kenneth Doka named the losses society fails to validate:
- Ex-partners, affairs
- Pet loss
- Perinatal loss, miscarriage
- Estranged family members
- Loss of a future (infertility, career loss)

When grief is disenfranchised, the person lacks social support, rituals, and permission to grieve — adding isolation to loss.

## Attachment Predicts Grief

[Attachment style](../concepts/attachment-theory.md) shapes how people grieve:

| Attachment | Grief Pattern |
|---|---|
| **Secure** | Painful but adaptive; can oscillate between loss and restoration |
| **Anxious-preoccupied** | Chronic grief; difficulty shifting to restoration-orientation |
| **Dismissive-avoidant** | Delayed or absent grief; may surface later as somatic symptoms |
| **Fearful-avoidant** | Complicated, disorganized grief; oscillation is chaotic |

## Growth After Loss

[Post-traumatic growth](../concepts/post-traumatic-growth.md) after bereavement — deepened relationships, new priorities, greater appreciation for life — is documented by Tedeschi & Calhoun. Growth and grief coexist; finding meaning doesn't mean the loss was "worth it."
