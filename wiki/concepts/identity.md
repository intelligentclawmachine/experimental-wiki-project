# Identity and the Self

The sense of who you are — your values, commitments, life story, and continuity over time. Identity is not fixed at birth or settled in adolescence; it is constructed, revised, and sometimes shattered across the lifespan.

## Erikson: Identity vs. Role Confusion

Erik Erikson proposed that the fifth psychosocial stage (adolescence) centers on **identity formation** — consolidating a coherent sense of self through exploration and commitment. Failure produces role confusion: not knowing who you are or what you stand for. Erikson coined "identity crisis" — a period of active searching that, while painful, is necessary for growth. (Source: [Identity and the Self](../sources/identity-self.md))

## Marcia's Four Identity Statuses

James Marcia operationalized Erikson's ideas along two dimensions — exploration and commitment:

| Status | Exploration | Commitment | Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Diffusion** | Low | Low | Drifting, no direction |
| **Foreclosure** | Low | High | Adopted without question (e.g., parents' values) |
| **Moratorium** | High | Low | Actively searching, not yet committed |
| **Achievement** | High | High | Explored and committed |

Achievement is consistently linked to the best psychological outcomes. Foreclosure looks stable on the surface but is brittle — it hasn't been tested. This maps directly to [ego-syntonic](../concepts/ego-syntonic-and-dystonic.md) patterns: foreclosed identities feel natural but may not be authentic.

## Narrative Identity

Dan McAdams argues that from late adolescence onward, we construct **internalized life stories** that give our existence unity and purpose. Two key narrative patterns:

- **Redemption sequences** — bad events lead to good outcomes ("I suffered, but it made me stronger"). Characteristic of psychologically healthy adults
- **Contamination sequences** — good events are spoiled by negative turns ("Things were going well, but then..."). Correlate with depression and lower well-being

Your identity is not just what happens to you — it is **the story you tell about what happens to you**. This connects to [post-traumatic growth](../concepts/post-traumatic-growth.md): growth requires constructing a redemption narrative from adversity.

## Self-Concept Clarity

Jennifer Campbell's research shows that **clearly defined, consistent, stable self-beliefs** predict well-being independently of self-esteem. You can have high self-esteem but low clarity ("I feel good about myself, but I don't know who I am"). Low self-concept clarity is associated with neuroticism, [rumination](../concepts/rumination.md), and poor adjustment.

## Possible Selves

Hazel Markus & Paula Nurius: we carry cognitive representations of **hoped-for selves** (what we want to become) and **feared selves** (what we dread becoming). These serve as motivational resources — pulling us toward desired futures and pushing us away from dreaded ones. The gap between current and possible selves drives both [motivation](../concepts/motivation.md) and [regret](../concepts/regret.md).

## Identity Disruption

Identity is most vulnerable during:
- **Trauma** — shatters assumptions about self and world
- **Major transitions** — divorce, job loss, retirement, parenthood
- **Midlife reassessment** — the "unlived life" reckoning
- **Loss** — [grief](../concepts/grief-and-loss.md) disrupts identity when the deceased was central to self-definition
