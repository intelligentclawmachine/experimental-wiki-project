# Psychological Richness

A third dimension of well-being beyond happiness and meaning, proposed by [[erin-westgate|Erin Westgate]] and [[shigehiro-oishi|Shigehiro Oishi]] (2022). Defined as a life filled with diverse, interesting experiences that change one's perspective.

## The Three Dimensions of a Good Life

| Dimension | Core | Feels Like | Example |
|-----------|------|-----------|---------|
| **Happiness** (hedonic) | Pleasure, comfort | Satisfied, content | Relaxing vacation |
| **Meaning** (eudaimonic) | Purpose, significance | Fulfilled, directed | Raising children |
| **Psychological Richness** | Diversity, perspective change | Fascinated, challenged | Living abroad in an unfamiliar culture |

## What Counts as "Rich"?

- Both external (travel, new challenges, career changes) and internal (great novels, haunting songs, deep conversations)
- Need not be pleasant or purposeful — a hurricane, a difficult move, a crisis can be "rich"
- "A psychologically rich life can come from something as simple as reading a great novel"
- Those pursuing **service to others** had the strongest sense of psychological richness

## Why This Matters

Before this framework, well-being research assumed people primarily want either happiness or meaning. Some people don't — they want *depth, variety, and growth through experience.* This explains why someone might choose a challenging, uncomfortable path (moving countries, changing careers, confronting hard truths) over a comfortable or even purposeful one.

## Trade-offs

"Interesting experiences aren't always pleasant experiences." People ideally want all three dimensions, but when forced to choose, some consistently prioritize richness over comfort or even purpose.

## Cross-Cultural Validity

The concept resonates across cultures, suggesting it captures something fundamental about human motivation, not just a Western preference.
