# Trust Repair

Trust repair is any increase in trust above the post-transgression level. It is not about returning to pre-betrayal innocence — that's gone. It's about building something new on the wreckage of what was broken.

## Why Trust Breaks

Trust breaks through two fundamentally different mechanisms, and the type of break determines the repair path (Source: [Trust Repair Review](../sources/trust-repair-review.md)):

### Competence Violations
"I failed because I couldn't." Dropped the ball, made a mistake, lacked skill.
- Feels like disappointment, not betrayal
- Relatively repairable — demonstrated improvement rebuilds confidence
- Best repair: own the gap, show you're improving ("I wasn't prepared enough")

### Integrity Violations
"I failed because I chose to." Deception, dishonesty, broken commitments.
- Feels like genuine betrayal — a moral violation
- Much harder to repair — **trust damaged by deception never fully recovers**
- Best repair: acknowledge the wrong, explain context, demonstrate changed behavior over time

This maps to Hawley's philosophical insight: trust involves reliance on someone's *commitments*. Competence failure disappoints; integrity failure *betrays*. (Source: [Philosophy of Trust](../sources/philosophy-of-trust.md))

## The Deception Ceiling

The most sobering finding in the trust repair literature: when the violation involved deliberate deception, trust has a ceiling it can never again reach — even with sincere apologies, consistent behavior change, and robust accountability structures. The knowledge that "you lied to me" creates a permanent adjustment to the mental model. (Source: [Trust Repair Review](../sources/trust-repair-review.md))

## Five Repair Strategies

From the couples therapy literature (Giacobbi 2025):

1. **Proactive transparency** — the violator volunteers information rather than waiting to be asked. This reverses the information asymmetry that deception created.
2. **Active monitoring** — agreed-upon accountability structures. Paradoxically, monitoring *supports* trust repair by making trustworthiness verifiable.
3. **Remorse and accountability** — genuine expression that demonstrates understanding of the harm, not just regret at getting caught.
4. **Shared activities** — rebuilding a bank of positive experiences (echoes [[john-gottman|Gottman's]] 5:1 ratio).
5. **Clear communication of reasons** — explaining *why* it happened reduces the threat of recurrence.

## What Doesn't Work

- **Denial** — denying a violation when evidence exists destroys remaining trust. It adds deception to the original violation.
- **Forgiveness alone** — intrapersonal forgiveness (letting go of resentment) is necessary but not sufficient. Without interpersonal repair processes, forgiveness doesn't restore the relationship.
- **Time alone** — "time heals all wounds" is false for trust. Without active repair, trust deteriorates further or calcifies into permanent distrust.

## Trust Repair and Forgiveness

[[forgiveness|Forgiveness]] and trust repair are related but distinct:

- **Forgiveness** is internal — releasing resentment and the desire for retribution
- **Trust repair** is relational — rebuilding confidence in the other person's future behavior
- You can forgive without restoring trust (and often should)
- You cannot restore trust without some degree of forgiveness

## Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT)

The most evidence-based approach to trust repair in couples is the Attachment Injury Resolution Model within EFT (Source: [EFT/AIRM](../sources/eft-attachment-injury-resolution.md)):

1. **De-escalate** the negative cycle — help both partners see the *pattern* as the enemy, not each other
2. **Restructure** interactions — the injured partner expresses vulnerable emotions (fear, grief) underneath anger; the injuring partner offers genuine emotional presence, not just cognitive acknowledgment
3. **Consolidate** — new patterns of accessibility and responsiveness become self-reinforcing

EFT has a 70-75% success rate and lasting improvements at 2-year follow-up. It's grounded in [[attachment-theory]]: betrayal is an attachment injury that shatters the sense of the relationship as a safe haven. Repair means re-establishing the partner as a secure base.

## The Neuroscience of Repair

Trust repair requires overcoming the brain's threat detection system. After betrayal, the amygdala is primed to detect danger from the violator. Repair works by gradually building new positive associations that compete with the threat signal — similar to how exposure therapy works for phobias. Oxytocin from small positive interactions gradually dampens the cortisol response. (Source: [Neuroscience of Trust](../sources/neuroscience-of-trust.md))
