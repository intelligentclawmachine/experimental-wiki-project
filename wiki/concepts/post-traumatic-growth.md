# Post-Traumatic Growth

Post-traumatic growth (PTG) is the experience of significant positive change arising from the struggle with a major life crisis. It is not the absence of suffering but transformation *through* suffering.

## The Fork in the Road

When trauma shatters fundamental assumptions — about identity, safety, justice, predictability — the disorientation creates a critical choice point:

- **Avoidance path** → [experiential avoidance](../concepts/experiential-avoidance.md) → emotional numbing → PTSD maintenance → stagnation
- **Engagement path** → deliberate reflection → schema reconstruction → growth

The determining factor is whether the person can tolerate the discomfort of processing — which is why [psychological flexibility](../sources/act-transdiagnostic.md), [secure attachment](../concepts/attachment-theory.md), and [social support](../concepts/relationships.md) all predict PTG. (Source: [Tedeschi](../sources/post-traumatic-growth.md))

## Five Domains of Growth

1. **Improved relationships** — deeper intimacy, greater empathy, stronger connections
2. **New possibilities** — paths and interests previously unconsidered
3. **Greater life appreciation** — heightened gratitude, savoring of the ordinary
4. **Enhanced personal strength** — "I didn't know I could survive this"
5. **Spiritual/existential depth** — deeper engagement with questions of meaning and purpose

## Intrusive vs. Deliberate Rumination

Not all rumination is pathological. The key distinction:

- **Intrusive rumination** — automatic, unwanted, distressing replay (common in early aftermath; a normal processing attempt)
- **Deliberate rumination** — intentional reflection on changed beliefs, meaning, and life narrative (associated with growth)

The therapeutic goal is to facilitate the *transition* from intrusive to deliberate — not to stop rumination altogether.

## Growth and Distress Coexist

A crucial nuance: PTG doesn't mean trauma is "over" or that suffering was worthwhile. People can experience significant growth *while still having PTSD symptoms*. Growth and distress are not opposites on a single dimension — they coexist in complex, non-linear ways.

## The Expert Companionship Model

Tedeschi's clinical approach emphasizes companionship over treatment:

1. **Trauma education** — normalizing responses, explaining the growth model
2. **Emotional regulation** — building distress tolerance (connects to [vagal tone](../concepts/vagal-tone-stress-regulation.md))
3. **Narrative disclosure** — telling the story in a safe environment
4. **Life narrative development** — integrating trauma into a coherent story
5. **Service-oriented direction** — channeling growth outward through helping others

## Connections

PTG connects to several wiki themes:

- It represents [meaning-focused coping](../concepts/stress-coping-models.md) in action
- It requires the opposite of [experiential avoidance](../concepts/experiential-avoidance.md)
- It deepens [psychological richness](../concepts/psychological-richness.md) — a life with PTG is a more complex, textured life
- It parallels movement up [Vaillant's defense hierarchy](../concepts/defense-mechanisms.md) — from immature (avoidance) to mature (sublimation, altruism)
- [Forgiveness](../concepts/forgiveness.md) often emerges as part of post-trauma reconstruction
