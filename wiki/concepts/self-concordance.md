# Self-Concordance

The degree to which personal goals align with intrinsic interests and core values — determining whether goal pursuit enhances or undermines well-being.

## The Model (Sheldon & Elliot, 1999)

Self-concordant goals are driven by autonomous motivation (identified importance + intrinsic interest). They produce more sustained effort, higher achievement, and greater well-being upon attainment.

### The Motivation Spectrum

| Type | Voice | Category | Well-being |
|------|-------|----------|-----------|
| **External** | "They want me to" | Controlled | Low |
| **Introjected** | "I should / I'd feel guilty" | Controlled | Low |
| **Identified** | "This matters to me" | Autonomous | High |
| **Intrinsic** | "I love this" | Autonomous | Highest |

## Identity Must Come First

Longitudinal research (2024): ego identity → self-concordance (**β = 0.21**), but NOT the reverse. **You must know who you are before you can set goals aligned with who you are.**

People with identity diffusion (lowest development) score 0.23 on self-concordance. People with identity achievement (highest) score 2.77. The gap is enormous.

## Why People Pursue Discordant Goals

If we have free will, why pursue goals misaligned with our values? The research answer: **insufficient identity development.** Without a clear sense of self, you're vulnerable to:
- Social pressure ("everyone does this")
- Introjected guilt ("I should want this")
- External rewards ("this pays well")
- Cultural norms ("this is what success looks like")

These goals feel [[ego-syntonic-and-dystonic|ego-syntonic]] — natural and chosen — while actually being controlled.

## The Self-Concordance / Ego-Syntonicity Distinction

This is the crucial insight connecting these domains:

**Ego-syntonic** = feels consistent with self-image (comfort axis)
**Self-concordant** = aligned with authentic values and intrinsic interests (values axis)

A behavior can be ego-syntonic (comfortable) WITHOUT being self-concordant (values-aligned). Examples:
- Working 80-hour weeks feels "like me" but violates your value of family time
- People-pleasing feels natural but serves introjected guilt, not genuine care
- Avoidance feels safe but contradicts your value of connection

**The dangerous pattern**: ego-syntonic + not self-concordant. It feels right but isn't.

## Cross-Cultural Validity

"Self-concordance predicted subjective well-being within every culture tested." Owning your actions — feeling goals are consistent with the self — appears universal.

## Three Basic Needs (SDT Foundation)

Self-concordant goals satisfy three basic needs:
1. **Autonomy** — acting from genuine self (connects to [[self-trust|self-trust]])
2. **Competence** — developing mastery (connects to self-efficacy)
3. **Relatedness** — feeling connected (connects to [[relationships|relationships]])

## Practical Implications

- **Clarify identity before setting goals** — journaling, therapy, values exercises
- **Test motivation type**: ask "Why am I doing this?" If the answer is "I should" or "they expect," examine whether it's truly yours
- **Notice the discomfort of authentic goals** — self-concordant goals may initially feel ego-dystonic if you've been pursuing discordant ones
- **Past crises don't predict self-concordance** — resolution matters, not the crisis itself
- **[[hope|Hope]]** is the emotion that makes self-concordant goals feel possible
