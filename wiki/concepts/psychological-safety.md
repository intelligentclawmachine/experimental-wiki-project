# Psychological Safety

A shared belief that a team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking. Not the absence of conflict, but the presence of permission — permission to ask questions, admit mistakes, propose ideas, and challenge without fear of punishment.

## Safety Is Not Trust (But They're Related)

A critical distinction (Source: [Edmondson](../sources/psychological-safety-edmondson.md)):

- **Psychological safety** is a *group climate* — "how things work here"
- **[[Trust]]** is a *dyadic relationship* — "can I count on you"

Safety is the soil; trust is the plant. You can't build deep interpersonal trust in an environment where people fear speaking up. And a psychologically safe environment naturally generates the repeated positive interactions from which trust grows.

## The Error-Reporting Paradox

Edmondson's landmark finding: the best hospital teams reported **more errors**, not fewer. Because they felt safe reporting mistakes, they learned faster and actually performed better.

Teams with low safety hid errors, creating a dangerous illusion of competence. The paradox: a team that looks flawless may be the most dysfunctional.

This extends to any context — relationships, organizations, communities. Where people can't safely acknowledge problems, problems don't disappear — they go underground.

## How Safety Is Built

Leaders (and relationship partners) build safety by:

1. **Framing as learning** — "we're here to learn, not to be perfect"
2. **Modeling fallibility** — "I may miss something, I need your input"
3. **Asking questions** — curiosity rather than certainty
4. **Responding well to failure** — treating mistakes as data, not crimes

This parallels [[john-gottman|Gottman's]] turning-toward research: how you respond to bids for connection (or admissions of failure) determines whether the environment feels safe.

## Safety and Vulnerability

Psychological safety is collective permission for [[vulnerability]]. In safe teams:
- Asking "I don't understand" is valued, not judged
- Saying "I was wrong" is modeled from the top
- Proposing risky ideas is encouraged, not punished

This maps directly to [[brene-brown|Brown's]] research: vulnerability looks like courage to others, even though it feels like weakness to you. Safety reduces the felt cost of vulnerability.

## The Neuroscience

Psychological safety operates through the same neurochemistry as individual trust (Source: [Neuroscience of Trust](../sources/neuroscience-of-trust.md)):
- Safe environments reduce cortisol (stress hormone)
- Reduced cortisol permits oxytocin release (trust hormone)
- Oxytocin motivates reciprocal vulnerability and cooperation
- The cycle compounds: more safety → more trust → more safety

Unsafe environments do the opposite: chronic vigilance → elevated cortisol → suppressed oxytocin → guarded behavior → less trust → less safety.
