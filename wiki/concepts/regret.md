# Regret

A cognitively sophisticated emotion that requires counterfactual reasoning — the ability to simulate alternative outcomes and recognize that a different choice would have led to a better result. Regret is not merely feeling bad; it is feeling bad *because you can see a better path you didn't take*.

## The Neural Signature

The orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) is essential for regret. Camille et al. (2004) showed that patients with OFC lesions experience disappointment (a bad outcome) but not regret (recognizing a different choice would have been better). Regret requires *comparison* — it is a higher-order emotion, not just negative affect. (Source: [The Psychology of Regret](../sources/regret-psychology.md))

## Day-to-Day Regret

### The Temporal Pattern: Actions vs. Inactions

Gilovich & Medvec (1995) discovered a fundamental asymmetry:

| Timeframe | Dominant Regret | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Short-term** | Actions (things done wrong) | Vivid, concrete, emotionally intense |
| **Long-term** | Inactions (things left undone) | Unbounded "what if" space, no closure |

Actions produce discrete, bounded outcomes that people adapt to and rationalize. Inactions leave open an infinite space of "what might have been." In surveys of older adults, inaction regrets outnumber action regrets roughly 2:1.

### Counterfactual Thinking

Regret is powered by counterfactual simulation (Kahneman & Tversky, 1982):

- **Upward counterfactuals** ("it could have been better") — generate regret, dissatisfaction, but can motivate correction
- **Downward counterfactuals** ("it could have been worse") — generate relief, serve a palliative function

The brain defaults to upward counterfactuals after negative outcomes (Roese, 1997). The **near-miss effect** intensifies regret: outcomes that *almost* turned out differently produce the sharpest pain.

### Anticipated Regret

People don't just experience regret retrospectively — they anticipate it and let it shape decisions. Regret aversion theory (Zeelenberg, 1999; Loomes & Sugden, 1982): decision-makers choose options that minimize potential future regret, even when this conflicts with expected utility. This drives:
- Risk aversion and insurance purchases
- Inaction bias (choosing the status quo to avoid regret of a bad active choice)
- Overdeliberation and decision paralysis

### Choice Overload and the Paradox of Choice

Barry Schwartz (*The Paradox of Choice*, 2004): excessive options amplify regret through more counterfactual comparisons and greater opportunity cost.

**Maximizers** (who seek the best option) vs. **satisficers** (who seek "good enough"):
- Maximizers experience more regret, more social comparison, less satisfaction
- Satisficers make faster decisions and feel better about them

Iyengar & Lepper's jam study (2000): 24 varieties of jam attracted more browsers but fewer buyers than 6 varieties. More choices, more regret, less action.

## Lifetime Regret

### What People Regret Most

Roese & Summerville (2005) meta-analyzed the research: **education** is the most commonly cited life regret, followed by career, romance, parenting, self-improvement, and finance. The key finding: regret concentrates where people perceive the most opportunity — you regret most in domains where you feel you *could have* acted differently.

### Pink's Four Core Regrets

Daniel Pink (*The Power of Regret*, 2022) surveyed 16,000+ people across 105 countries and found four structural categories:

| Regret Type | Core Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Foundation** | Failing to be responsible, build stability | "I should have studied harder / saved more" |
| **Boldness** | Failing to take risks | "I should have started that business / asked them out" |
| **Moral** | Failing to do the right thing | "I should have stood up for them" |
| **Connection** | Failing to maintain relationships | "I should have stayed in touch" |

**Boldness regrets predominated** — aligning with Gilovich & Medvec's finding that inaction regrets dominate over time.

### Regrets of the Dying

Bronnie Ware, a palliative care nurse, documented five recurring regrets:
1. Not living true to oneself
2. Working too hard
3. Not expressing feelings
4. Losing touch with friends
5. Not allowing oneself more happiness

These map closely to Pink's boldness and connection categories, and point directly at [self-concordance](../concepts/self-concordance.md) — the regret of living someone else's life.

### Regret and Aging

Brassen et al. (2012): healthy older adults show **reduced regret sensitivity** — an adaptive emotional regulation pattern. But depressed older adults show regret responses resembling younger adults, suggesting failed regret regulation contributes to late-life depression. Successful aging involves learning to let go of counterfactual possibilities.

## Regret as a Value Signal

The most important reframe in regret research: **regrets reveal what you care about**. If you regret not being bolder, you value courage. If you regret lost connections, you value [relationships](../concepts/relationships.md). If you regret inauthenticity, you value [self-concordance](../concepts/self-concordance.md).

This connects to Sheldon's self-concordance theory: people most regret paths misaligned with their authentic goals — the "unlived life" phenomenon. Regret is the emotional signal of value misalignment.

## Regret Regulation

Zeelenberg & Pieters (2007) identified strategies at three levels:

- **Decision-focused** — switching, reversing choices (when possible)
- **Alternative-focused** — devaluing rejected options, downward counterfactuals ("at least...")
- **Feeling-focused** — psychological distancing, silver-lining thinking, self-justification

### Self-Compassion as Regret Regulation

Kristin Neff's [self-compassion](../concepts/self-compassion.md) framework, applied to regret, reduces rumination by replacing self-criticism with common humanity recognition. Zhang & Chen (2016): self-compassion interventions reduced regret-related distress without undermining motivation to improve. You can be gentle with yourself about the past while still learning from it.

### When Regret Becomes Toxic

Regret turns destructive when it becomes ruminative — repetitive, self-blaming, and disconnected from action. This is [experiential avoidance](../concepts/experiential-avoidance.md) in reverse: rather than avoiding the feeling, the person becomes trapped in it. The ACT perspective: the goal is not to eliminate regret but to *process* it as information and redirect toward valued action.
