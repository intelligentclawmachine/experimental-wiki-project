# The Top 10 Insights from the "Science of a Meaningful Life" in 2024

Source: Greater Good Science Center, UC Berkeley
Authors: Kira M. Newman, Jill Suttie, Emiliana R. Simon-Thomas, Maryam Abdullah, Elizabeth Hopper, Edward Lempinen, Jeremy Adam Smith
Date: December 19, 2024
URL: https://greatergood.berkeley.edu/article/item/the_top_10_insights_from_the_science_of_a_meaningful_life_in_2024

## 1. Indigenous Cultures Offer Overlooked Happiness Lessons
A 2024 study by Eric Galbraith (McGill University), published in *PNAS*, surveyed nearly 3,000 people across 19 small communities in Asia, Africa, and South America. Participants reported life satisfaction of 6.8/10 despite living on less than $1,000 annually — substantially higher than people earning $25,000+ in global surveys. Village location predicted satisfaction independent of wealth. Non-economic factors matter: interdependent community living, nature proximity, and lower inequality. "Small-scale societies living in close contact with nature...offer distinctly valuable perspectives."

## 2. Old Friends Present Untapped Connection Opportunities
Research by Lara B. Aknin and Gillian M. Sandstrom across six studies involving 2,500+ participants from the U.S., U.K., and Canada revealed fewer than one-third contacted old friends despite believing reconnection would be appreciated. Practicing outreach with current friends first increased likelihood of contacting distant acquaintances.

## 3. 20-Second Self-Compassion Practice Reduces Stress
A study by Eli Susman published in *Behavior Research and Therapy* assigned 135 young adults to either self-compassionate touch (hand on heart, opposite hand on belly) or dexterity exercises for 20 seconds daily over one month. The self-compassion group showed significantly greater self-compassion and reduced stress, anxiety, and depression. "Daily micropractices have the potential for augmenting single-session interventions."

## 4. Biodiversity Boosts Mental Health
Research by Ryan Hammoud (King's College London) using the Urban Mind App tracked nearly 2,000 people worldwide over two weeks. People experienced greater mental well-being when observing natural elements (trees, plants, birds, water), with effects lasting up to eight hours. Each additional natural element yielded an average 0.91-point mental health increase (on 50-point scale). "Moving away from monocultural pockets...towards polycultural spaces which mimic natural ecosystems."

## 5. Forgiveness Training Benefits Mental Health Globally
Two large 2024 studies: (1) Nearly 4,600 participants in conflict-affected regions (Hong Kong, Indonesia, Ukraine, Colombia, South Africa) completed REACH forgiveness framework training — reduced depression and anxiety. (2) A Colombian university campus-wide campaign with nearly 2,900 students showed increased willingness to forgive and decreased depression and anxiety.

## 6. Empathy Transmits Across Three Generations
A 25-year longitudinal study by Jessica Stern published in *Child Development*: Mothers' empathy toward 13-year-old children predicted teen empathy toward close friends. Teens' friendship empathy predicted their later parental empathy. Parents' empathy toward children predicted children's empathic tendencies. Empathy serves as a "training ground" through adolescent friendships.

## 7. Intellectually Humble Teachers Improve Student Learning
Research by Tenelle Porter, Mark R. Leary, and Andrei Cimpian: approximately 300 high school students found that perceiving teachers as intellectually humble led to greater intellectual humility, classroom acceptance, increased interest, and improved grades. Modeling humility proved more effective than explicitly teaching it.

## 8. Exposure to Misinformation Can "Inoculate" Children
Research published in *Nature Human Behaviour*: children ages 4-7 exposed to earlier inaccuracies demonstrated greater skepticism about new claims. "Efforts to prevent misinformation should focus on helping children develop...skill sets for evaluating information."

## 9. Disagreement Conversations Aren't As Unpleasant As Imagined
Kristina Wald found people systematically underestimate pleasant aspects of conversations with disagreeing strangers. Actual conversations proved far more enjoyable than predicted. A companion study by Erica Bailey revealed "Three forces — salience of online debates, amplification of negative content, and negativity bias — have together warped perceptions of debate."

## 10. Research-Tested Democracy Preservation Strategies Exist
A mega-study in *Science* by Jan G. Voelkel (Cornell) and Robb Willer (Stanford) tested 25 solutions across 32,000+ participants. The most effective intervention corrected misconceptions about opponents' democratic hostility by presenting public opinion data. "People should be very happy to hear that voters on all sides really do support democracy."
