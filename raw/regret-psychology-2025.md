# The Psychology of Regret (Research Synthesis, 2025)

## Microscale / Day-to-Day Regret

### Action vs. Inaction
Gilovich & Medvec (1995): Short-term, action regrets dominate. Long-term, inaction regrets grow and take over (~2:1 ratio in older adults). Actions produce bounded outcomes that people rationalize; inactions leave open unbounded "what might have been" space.

### Counterfactual Thinking
Kahneman & Tversky (1982): Regret powered by counterfactual simulation. Upward counterfactuals ("could have been better") generate regret but motivate correction. Downward counterfactuals ("could have been worse") generate relief. Roese (1997): brain defaults to upward after negative outcomes. "Near miss" effect: outcomes that nearly turned out differently produce sharpest regret.

### Neural Basis
Camille et al. (2004): Orbitofrontal cortex essential for regret. OFC lesion patients experience disappointment but not regret — regret requires counterfactual comparison. Coricelli et al. (2005): OFC and amygdala activation during regret processing.

### Anticipated Regret
Zeelenberg (1999), Loomes & Sugden (1982): Regret aversion theory — people choose to minimize potential future regret, even against expected utility. Drives risk aversion, insurance purchases, inaction bias.

### Choice Overload
Schwartz, The Paradox of Choice (2004): Excessive options amplify regret through more counterfactual comparisons. Maximizers vs. satisficers — maximizers experience more regret, more social comparison, less satisfaction. Iyengar & Lepper (2000): jam study — more choices, fewer purchases, lower satisfaction.

## Lifetime / Long-Term Regret

### Domains
Roese & Summerville (2005): Education is #1 life regret, then career, romance, parenting, self-improvement, finance. Regret concentrates where people perceive most opportunity for corrective action.

### Pink's Four Core Regrets
Daniel Pink, The Power of Regret (2022), 16,000+ people across 105 countries:
1. Foundation regrets — failing to be responsible, build stability
2. Boldness regrets — failing to take risks (predominant category)
3. Moral regrets — failing to do the right thing
4. Connection regrets — failing to maintain relationships

### Regrets of the Dying
Bronnie Ware, palliative care nurse:
1. Not living true to oneself
2. Working too hard
3. Not expressing feelings
4. Losing touch with friends
5. Not allowing oneself more happiness

### Regret and Aging
Brassen et al. (2012): Healthy older adults show reduced regret sensitivity — adaptive emotional regulation. Depressed older adults show regret responses like younger adults — failed regret regulation contributes to late-life depression.

### Regret-Meaning Connection
Regrets reveal values. Sheldon's self-concordance: people most regret paths misaligned with authentic goals — the "unlived life."

## Mechanisms and Interventions

### Regret Regulation
Zeelenberg & Pieters (2007): Three levels:
- Decision-focused (switching, reversing)
- Alternative-focused (devaluing rejected options, downward counterfactuals)
- Feeling-focused (psychological distancing, silver-lining, self-justification)

### Self-Compassion
Neff's framework applied to regret reduces rumination. Zhang & Chen (2016): self-compassion interventions reduce regret-related distress without undermining motivation.

### Regret as Information
Regret serves as value-clarification signal. Processing it reveals commitments and priorities. Risk: when regret becomes ruminative (repetitive, self-blaming, disconnected from action) it feeds depressive cycles. Goal: transform regret from suffering into motivational data.
