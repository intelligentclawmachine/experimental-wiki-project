## The Social Brain Hypothesis

Robin Dunbar (1990s) found a correlation between primate brain size and average social group size. Primates evolved large brains to support complex social lives. Time spent grooming correlates with group size, which correlates with brain size.

## Dunbar's Number: ~150

Humans can comfortably maintain approximately 150 stable social relationships — defined as knowing who each person is and how they relate to everyone else.

## Layers of Intimacy (Scaling by ~3x)

Human social networks are organized into concentric circles, each ~3x the size of the one inside it:

| Layer | Size | Contact | Description |
|-------|------|---------|-------------|
| **Support clique** | ~5 | Weekly | Closest relationships; people you turn to in crisis |
| **Sympathy group** | ~15 | Monthly | Close friends you'd miss if you couldn't see them |
| **Active network** | ~50 | Periodically | People you'd stop and chat with if you bumped into them |
| **Dunbar's number** | ~150 | Occasionally | Stable social relationships; meaningful connections |
| **Acquaintances** | ~500 | Rarely | People you recognize and can place |
| **Recognizable faces** | ~1,500 | — | People you can identify |

## Key Findings

- Each layer demands different amounts of cognitive and emotional investment, as well as time and energy
- More than a dozen neuroimaging studies show social network size correlates with the size of the default mode neural network (the brain circuit managing social relationships)
- The layered structure appears in both personal networks and communities
- Relationship maintenance is a zero-sum game for time — investing more in one layer means less for others

## Research Support (2025)

A PLOS ONE study (2025) on individual differences in energy allocation to personal relationships confirmed Dunbar's layered structure while noting significant individual variation in how people distribute emotional investment across layers.

## Implications

- Relationships have cognitive limits — you cannot maintain unlimited deep connections
- The ~5 closest relationships matter most for well-being (maps to Harvard Study findings)
- Meaningful life requires investing in the inner circles, not maximizing the outer ones
- Quality over quantity is not just advice — it's a cognitive constraint
