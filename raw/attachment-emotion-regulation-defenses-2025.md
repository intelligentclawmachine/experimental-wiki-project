# Attachment, Emotion Regulation, and Defense Mechanisms: An Integrative Review

**Source:** Morris et al. (2025). "Attachment, Emotion Regulation, and Defense Mechanisms: An Integrative Review." PMC/NIH.
**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12691835/

## Overview

This 2025 narrative review integrates three constructs — attachment theory, emotion regulation, and defense mechanisms — into a unified framework, mapping specific defenses to each attachment style.

## Deactivating vs. Hyperactivating Strategies

### Avoidant-Dismissing (Deactivating)
Individuals with dismissive-avoidant attachment use **deactivating strategies** to minimize emotional engagement:
- Suppression of emotions
- Emotional distancing
- Denial of vulnerability or need
- Intellectualization (thinking about feelings rather than feeling them)
- Compulsive self-reliance

### Anxious-Preoccupied (Hyperactivating)
Individuals with anxious-preoccupied attachment use **hyperactivating strategies** to maximize proximity-seeking:
- Splitting (idealization/devaluation cycles)
- Projective identification
- Acting out
- Emotional amplification
- Hypervigilance to signs of rejection

## The Avoidant Affect-Arousal Mismatch

A critical finding: avoidant individuals show a **mismatch between subjective and physiological responses**:
- They report feeling calm and unaffected
- Their bodies register high physiological arousal (elevated cortisol, heart rate, skin conductance)
- This disconnect means avoidance "works" subjectively but fails physiologically — the body still pays the price

This has direct implications for allostatic load: avoidant individuals accumulate stress damage while believing they are coping well.

## Attachment Style → ER Strategy → Defense Mechanism Mapping

| Attachment Style | Primary ER Strategy | Typical Defenses | Behavioral Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| Secure | Flexible, context-appropriate | Mature (humor, sublimation, anticipation) | Seeks support when needed, self-regulates when able |
| Dismissive-Avoidant | Deactivating | Suppression, denial, intellectualization | "I'm fine, I don't need anyone" |
| Anxious-Preoccupied | Hyperactivating | Splitting, projection, acting out | Intense emotional expression, fear of abandonment |
| Fearful-Avoidant | Disorganized (switching) | Dissociation, passive aggression, somatization | Approach-avoid oscillation |

## Attachment Defenses Questionnaire

Richardson et al. (2025) developed a new clinical assessment tool — the Attachment Defenses Questionnaire — for identifying defense patterns linked to attachment styles in therapeutic settings.

## Clinical Implications

- Therapy should address not just the defense but the underlying attachment need it serves
- Avoidant clients may appear "fine" in therapy while physiologically dysregulated
- Defense maturation (moving up Vaillant's hierarchy) parallels movement toward earned security
- Understanding deactivating vs. hyperactivating patterns helps therapists choose appropriate interventions
