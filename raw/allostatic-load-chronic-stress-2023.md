# Allostatic Load and Allostatic Overload

**Source:** Pfaltz, Schnyder et al. (2023). "Allostatic Load and Allostatic Overload: Preventive and Clinical Implications." PMC/NIH.
**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10716872/

## Overview

This paper explains the biological mechanism by which chronic stress damages the body, introducing the concepts of allostasis, allostatic load, and allostatic overload.

## Core Concepts

- **Allostasis** — the brain's capacity to achieve stability through change; the active process of adapting to stressors by adjusting physiological parameters
- **Allostatic load** — the cumulative "wear and tear" on the body when stress response systems are chronically activated
- **Allostatic overload** — occurs when demands persistently exceed coping capacity, producing multi-system dysregulation and disease

## Clinical Staging Framework (Fava et al.)

- **Stage 0: Euthymia** — baseline well-being, adequate coping
- **Stage 1: Stress response** — normal physiological activation, recovery expected
- **Stage 2: Allostatic load accumulation** — subclinical dysregulation, biomarker changes detectable
- **Stage 3: Clinical illness onset** — frank disease emerges (cardiovascular, metabolic, psychiatric, immune)

## Five Biological Systems Affected

1. **HPA axis** (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal) — cortisol dysregulation, flattened diurnal curve
2. **Cardiovascular** — elevated resting blood pressure, accelerated atherosclerosis
3. **Immune/inflammatory** — chronic low-grade inflammation (elevated CRP, IL-6)
4. **Metabolic** — insulin resistance, visceral fat accumulation, metabolic syndrome
5. **Autonomic nervous system** — reduced heart rate variability, sympathetic dominance

## Early-Life Trauma and Developmental Allostatic Load

- Trauma during critical developmental periods initiates allostatic load with lifelong health consequences
- Adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) create a biological "head start" on wear-and-tear
- The younger the trauma exposure, the greater the cumulative load by adulthood

## Social Support as Buffer

- Social support inversely relates to allostatic load
- Strong relationships buffer the physiological stress response
- Social isolation is itself a source of allostatic load

## Interventions Showing Promise

- Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) — reduces cortisol and inflammatory markers
- Tai chi and mindfulness practices — lower sympathetic activation
- Group resilience training — reduces allostatic load parameters
- Exercise — one of the strongest buffers against allostatic overload
- Sleep hygiene — sleep disruption is a major contributor to allostatic load
