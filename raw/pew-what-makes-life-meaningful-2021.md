# What Makes Life Meaningful? Views From 17 Advanced Economies

Source: Pew Research Center
Authors: Laura Silver, Patrick van Kessel, Christine Huang, Laura Clancy, Sneha Gubbala
Date: November 18, 2021
URL: https://www.pewresearch.org/global/2021/11/18/what-makes-life-meaningful-views-from-17-advanced-economies/
Survey: 18,850 adults across 17 advanced economies (Feb. 1 to May 26, 2021)

## Key Findings

### Family as Primary Source of Meaning
In 14 of 17 economies, family ranks as the top source of meaning. Around half or more cite family in Australia, New Zealand, Greece, and the United States.

### Other Top Sources of Meaning
- **Careers/Occupations:** Range from 43% (Italy) to 6% (South Korea)
- **Material Well-Being:** One of top three factors in 9 of 17 publics; top source in South Korea
- **Health:** Top three factor in roughly one-third of places; 48% in Spain, 6% in Taiwan
- **Friends:** Significant across publics
- **Nature:** Notable emphasis in UK, Australia, France, New Zealand, Sweden

### Notable Country Variations
- Taiwan uniquely ranks "society/institutions" as top source, with citizens praising government services and pandemic response
- Spain emphasizes health most prominently
- South Korea: material well-being is top source of meaning

### Religion
Only the United States significantly emphasizes religion (15%), making it the fifth-most cited source. Outside America, no non-American public exceeded 5% mentioning faith.

### Demographic Patterns
- **Age:** Younger people prioritize friends, education, hobbies; older adults emphasize retirement and health
- **Gender:** Women slightly more likely to cite family and health than men
- **Education/Income:** Higher education and income correlate with citing family and career; lower-income individuals more likely to mention challenges
- **Political Ideology:** Left-leaning individuals cite nature, friends, hobbies more; right-leaning cite religion more; Republicans emphasize freedom and independence

### Challenges
A median of 10% across all publics mention difficulties interfering with meaning-seeking; ranges from ~5% (New Zealand, UK) to ~20% (Italy).
