# Stimulation as Avoidance: Active Escape from Internal States (Research Synthesis, 2025)

## Core Framework

Steven Hayes (ACT) defines experiential avoidance as unwillingness to remain in contact with aversive private experiences combined with deliberate efforts to alter or escape them. Critically, avoidance is functionally defined — it includes not just withdrawal but any behavior whose function is to suppress or escape internal states.

## Active Avoidance: Busyness as Escape

Traditional conceptions emphasize withdrawal — isolation, behavioral shutdown, passivity. But avoidance through action is equally prevalent. Adrian Wells' metacognitive model and Nolen-Hoeksema's research on distraction as coping both identify how activity redirects attention from unwanted affect.

Keeping perpetually busy, overworking, doom-scrolling, binge-watching: all serve the same functional role as withdrawal. Key difference: active avoiders appear engaged and functional, making avoidance harder to detect clinically.

## Emotional Numbing Through Overstimulation

Flooding the senses produces paradoxical numbing. Aligns with Damasio's Somatic Marker Hypothesis and sensory gating research. When nervous system is overwhelmed with input, interoceptive signals (body's emotional messengers) get crowded out.

Van der Kolk: some trauma survivors seek intense stimulation precisely because it overrides interoceptive awareness that would surface traumatic affect. Mechanism is attentional competition: external sensory load suppresses internally generated emotional signals.

## Behavioral Addictions as Avoidance

Process addictions — gambling, gaming, compulsive shopping, social media — activate same mesolimbic dopamine pathways as substance addictions (Potenza neuroimaging). Jon Grant and Samuel Chamberlain documented that negative reinforcement (relief from distress) is often a stronger driver than positive reinforcement (pleasure).

## Dopamine, Tolerance, and the Pleasure-Pain Balance

Dopamine encodes anticipation and novelty rather than pleasure per se (Sapolsky, Schultz). Stimulation-seeking avoidance exploits this: novel stimuli trigger dopamine release that temporarily overrides negative affect.

Tolerance develops — reward prediction error diminishes with repetition. Anna Lembke's pleasure-pain balance model (drawing on Solomon's opponent-process theory): chronic overstimulation downregulates dopamine receptors, producing baseline deficit that paradoxically increases the distress originally avoided. Self-reinforcing cycle.

## The ACT Perspective

All these behaviors represent psychological inflexibility — fusion with avoidance-driven rules and loss of values-directed action. Experiential avoidance regardless of form (active or passive) correlates with reduced wellbeing (Gloster et al.).

## The Paradox

Stimulation-based avoidance amplifies the problem it attempts to solve. Hedonic adaptation and dopaminergic tolerance demand escalating input. Meanwhile, avoided emotions remain unprocessed, often intensifying.
