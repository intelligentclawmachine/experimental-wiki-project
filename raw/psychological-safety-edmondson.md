# Psychological Safety and Learning in Organizations

**Source:** Amy Edmondson (1999). "Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work Teams." Administrative Science Quarterly.
**URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.2307/2666999

## Overview

Edmondson's foundational research introduces "team psychological safety" — a shared belief that a team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking — and empirically demonstrates its connection to learning behavior across 51 work teams.

## Psychological Safety Defined

"A shared belief held by members of a team that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking."

This means team members believe they can:
- Ask questions without being seen as ignorant
- Admit mistakes without being punished
- Propose ideas without being ridiculed
- Challenge the status quo without retaliation

## Psychological Safety vs. Trust

A critical distinction:

| | Psychological Safety | Interpersonal Trust |
|---|---|---|
| **Level** | Group/team property | Dyadic (between two people) |
| **Focus** | Willingness to take risks in the group | Willingness to be vulnerable with a specific person |
| **Scope** | Climate — "how things work here" | Relationship — "can I count on you" |
| **Mechanism** | Shared norms reduce fear | Accumulated experience builds confidence |

Both involve vulnerability, but psychological safety is about the team environment, while trust is about specific relationships.

## The Error-Reporting Paradox

Edmondson's landmark finding: the best hospital teams reported **more errors**, not fewer. Why? Because they felt safe reporting errors. Teams with low psychological safety hid errors, creating the illusion of competence while actually performing worse.

This insight extends beyond hospitals: in any context where learning matters, psychological safety predicts not fewer mistakes but faster learning from them.

## How Leaders Build Psychological Safety

1. **Frame work as a learning problem**, not an execution problem
2. **Acknowledge your own fallibility** — "I may miss something, I need to hear from you"
3. **Model curiosity** — ask lots of questions
4. **Respond productively to failure** — focus on what can be learned, not who to blame

## Connection to Trust

Psychological safety creates the conditions for trust to develop:
- Safe teams → members take interpersonal risks → positive responses → trust builds
- Unsafe teams → members self-censor → no vulnerability → trust stagnates
- Psychological safety is the *soil*; trust is the *plant* that grows in it
