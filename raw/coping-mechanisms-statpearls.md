# Coping Mechanisms — StatPearls Clinical Reference

**Source:** StatPearls (NCBI Bookshelf). "Coping Mechanisms." Continuously updated clinical reference.
**URL:** https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK559031/

## Overview

A comprehensive taxonomy of coping strategies and the theoretical models underlying them, drawing from clinical research and the Lazarus & Folkman transactional stress model.

## Lazarus & Folkman Transactional Model

The foundational model of stress and coping:

1. **Primary appraisal** — Is this situation threatening, harmful, or challenging?
2. **Secondary appraisal** — What can I do about it? Do I have the resources to cope?
3. **Coping response** — Selected based on the interaction of these two appraisals

Key insight: stress is not an inherent property of events but arises from the *relationship* between the person and the situation. The same event can be appraised as a threat or a challenge depending on perceived resources.

## Four Categories of Coping

### 1. Problem-Focused Coping
Aimed at directly changing the stressful situation:
- **Active coping** — taking direct action to address the problem
- **Planning** — thinking about steps and strategies
- **Restraint** — waiting for the right moment to act
- **Seeking instrumental support** — asking for practical help or advice

### 2. Emotion-Focused Coping
Aimed at managing emotional responses to stress:
- **Positive reframing** — finding a silver lining or growth opportunity
- **Acceptance** — acknowledging the reality of the situation
- **Humor** — finding levity in difficult circumstances
- **Turning to religion/spirituality** — seeking comfort in faith or meaning systems
- **Emotional support seeking** — talking to others for comfort and understanding

### 3. Meaning-Focused Coping
Aimed at deriving significance from stressful situations:
- Drawing on values, beliefs, and existential goals to reinterpret adversity
- Finding purpose or growth in suffering
- Related to Frankl's logotherapy and the search for meaning

### 4. Social Coping
Aimed at leveraging social resources:
- Seeking support from friends, family, community
- Social comparison (upward or downward)
- Communal coping — approaching the problem as "our" challenge

## Reactive vs. Proactive Coping

- **Reactive coping** — responding to stressors after they occur
- **Proactive coping** — anticipating future stressors and building resources in advance
- Evidence suggests proactive copers have better mental health outcomes

## Maladaptive Coping

- Behavioral disengagement (giving up)
- Mental disengagement (distraction, denial)
- Substance use
- Venting without problem-solving
- Self-blame

## Neurobiological Basis

- Serotonergic and dopaminergic activity in medial prefrontal cortex (PFC) underlies coping strategy selection
- Chronic maladaptive coping associated with HPA axis dysregulation

## Measurement Tools

- **COPE Inventory** (Carver, Scheier, & Weintraub, 1989) — 60 items, 15 scales
- **Ways of Coping Questionnaire** (Folkman & Lazarus, 1988)
- **Religious-COPE** — measures religious coping strategies specifically

## Health Outcomes

Maladaptive avoidant coping linked to:
- PTSD symptom maintenance
- Anxiety and depression
- Cardiovascular disease
- Poor treatment adherence in chronic illness
