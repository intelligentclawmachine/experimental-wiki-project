## Core Definitions

**Trust**: "Choosing to make something important to you vulnerable to the actions of someone else." (via Charles Feltman)

**Vulnerability**: "Uncertainty, risk, and emotional exposure." Not weakness — "our greatest measure of courage."

## The BRAVING Framework

Trust is composed of seven elements:

- **B — Boundaries**: Respecting your own and others' limits. You trust me when I set clear boundaries and respect yours.
- **R — Reliability**: Consistently doing what you say you'll do. Not overcommitting — being aware of your limitations.
- **A — Accountability**: Owning mistakes, apologizing, and making amends rather than blaming.
- **V — Vault**: Holding confidences. Not sharing information that isn't yours to share. Trust requires knowing shared information is safe.
- **I — Integrity**: Choosing courage over comfort; practicing your values rather than just professing them.
- **N — Non-judgment**: Allowing others (and yourself) to struggle and ask for help without judgment.
- **G — Generosity**: Assuming the most generous interpretation of others' words, intentions, and behaviors.

## The Marble Jar Metaphor

Trust builds incrementally through small, consistent moments — not grand gestures. Like a marble jar that fills one marble at a time. Connects to Gottman's "sliding door moments."

## Self-Trust (BRAVING Applied Inward)

Brown emphasizes that self-trust is foundational — you cannot trust others more than you trust yourself. The BRAVING framework applies to your relationship with yourself:
- Do I respect my own boundaries?
- Am I reliable to myself?
- Do I hold myself accountable?
- Do I keep my own confidences (not self-deprecating in public)?
- Do I act with integrity toward myself?
- Do I allow myself to struggle without self-judgment?
- Am I generous toward myself?

## Vulnerability and Connection

"Connection is why we're here. It's what gives purpose and meaning to our lives." Vulnerability is fundamental to forming deep, authentic relationships. When individuals allow themselves to be seen — flaws, fears, and all — they create opportunities for connection.

## Shame Resilience

Brown's research identifies shame as the primary barrier to vulnerability. Shame resilience involves: understanding vulnerabilities, cultivating empathy, courage, and compassion. Shame says "I am bad" (vs. guilt: "I did something bad"). Empathy is the antidote to shame.
