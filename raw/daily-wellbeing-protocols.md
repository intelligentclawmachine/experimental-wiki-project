# Daily Well-Being Protocols: Evidence-Based Compendium

Sources: Multiple searches across Huberman Lab, PMC, Harvard Health, Greater Good, Sleep Foundation, Psychology Today, Pew Research, Nature, Frontiers in Psychology, and others. Compiled 2026-04-05.

---

## 1. MORNING SUNLIGHT (Circadian Anchor)

**What**: Get outside for 5-30 minutes of sunlight within the first hour of waking.
**Evidence**: Morning sunlight triggers cortisol-awakening response, setting a timer for melatonin release 14-16 hours later. Suppresses melatonin (alertness now) and programs sleep later. Bright days: 5-10 min sufficient. Cloudy days: 15-20 min needed. Outdoor light (even cloudy) provides far more lux than indoor lighting.
**Mechanism**: Light → hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis → cortisol pulse → alertness. Also sets suprachiasmatic nucleus (master clock).
**Source**: Huberman Lab; Nature (circadian rhythm research); Oura Ring research review

## 2. CAFFEINE DELAY (90-120 min after waking)

**What**: Delay coffee/tea for 90-120 minutes after waking.
**Evidence**: Adenosine (sleepiness molecule) builds during sleep. Upon waking, adenosine is still present. Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors. If you drink caffeine immediately, it masks adenosine rather than clearing it — causing a mid-afternoon crash when caffeine wears off and adenosine floods back. Delaying allows natural adenosine clearance.
**Protocol**: Water + electrolytes upon waking. Caffeine at 90-120 min. Stop caffeine by noon to protect sleep.
**Source**: Huberman Lab protocols

## 3. EXERCISE (10-30+ minutes)

**What**: Any physical activity — walking, running, strength training, swimming.
**Evidence**:
- Short runs (10-20 min) reduce psychological distress and boost wellbeing
- 15-30 min moderate intensity produces lasting emotional benefits persisting beyond exercise
- Mood boost peaks immediately and lasts up to 3 hours
- Walking 9,800 steps/day reduces dementia risk by 50%
- Even 2,500 steps/day significantly reduces all-cause mortality
- Exercise is as effective as medication for mild-moderate depression
- Green exercise (outdoors) has additional meta-analytic benefits over indoor
**Minimum effective dose**: 10 minutes of any movement improves mood
**Optimal**: 20-30 min moderate intensity for sustained mood benefits
**Source**: Harvard Health; Frontiers in Psychology; PMC meta-analyses

## 4. COLD EXPOSURE (1-5 minutes, 2-4x/week)

**What**: Cold shower, ice bath, or cold water immersion.
**Evidence**:
- Increases dopamine by up to 250% (sustained, not spike-and-crash like stimulants)
- Increases norepinephrine and β-endorphin
- Participants felt more active, alert, attentive, proud, and inspired afterward
- 59% of regular cold-water swimmers reported reduced depressive symptoms
- Builds "top-down control" over stress responses — resilience that transfers to other domains
**Protocol**: 11 minutes total per week (2-4 sessions of 1-5 min). End showers cold for minimum dose.
**Caveat**: Research still developing; mixed study designs. Not appropriate for everyone.
**Source**: PMC (neurohormesis review); Huberman Lab; Journal of Neuropsychiatry

## 5. BREATHWORK (5 minutes)

**What**: Structured breathing practice, especially cyclic sighing.
**Evidence**: Stanford RCT (N=108, 28 days): 5-min daily cyclic sighing produced GREATER mood improvement than mindfulness meditation. Box breathing also effective.
- Cyclic sighing: +1.89 positive affect, -3.85 anxiety (p<0.0001)
- Mindfulness meditation: +1.22 positive affect (not significant), -3.95 anxiety
- Benefits compound with consistent practice
- Respiratory rate reduction correlated with mood improvement
**Protocols**:
- **Cyclic sighing** (best for mood): Double inhale through nose (fill lungs, then top off), long slow exhale through mouth. Repeat 5 min.
- **Box breathing** (good for focus): Equal inhale-hold-exhale-hold (3-7 sec each). Repeat 5 min.
- **Physiological sigh** (acute stress): One double-inhale + long exhale. 1-3 reps. Fastest real-time calm-down.
**Source**: Cell Reports Medicine (2023); Huberman Lab

## 6. MEDITATION (10-13 minutes)

**What**: Mindfulness meditation — focusing attention, observing without judgment.
**Evidence**:
- 10 min as effective as 20 min for state mindfulness improvement
- 8 weeks of 13-min daily practice: decreased negative mood, improved attention, working memory, recognition memory, decreased anxiety
- Meta-analyses show improvements in stress, cognition, and well-being
- Significant stress reduction after just 3 weeks
- Dose-response is not linear — moderate doses (10-20 min) capture most benefit
**Minimum effective dose**: 10 minutes daily
**Source**: PMC; Nature Scientific Reports; multiple meta-analyses

## 7. NATURE EXPOSURE (20-30 minutes)

**What**: Time in a natural environment — park, garden, forest, waterfront.
**Evidence**:
- 20-30 min is optimal dose for cortisol reduction (21% decrease in salivary cortisol)
- Even 10 min in urban nature improves mental health markers
- Each additional natural element (biodiversity) yields 0.91-point mental health increase
- Effects last up to 8 hours
- Interval exposure (multiple short doses) may be MORE effective than single long dose
- Nature "prescriptions" now used by universities and clinics
**Source**: Frontiers in Psychology; King's College London (biodiversity study); ScienceDaily

## 8. GRATITUDE PRACTICE (3-5 minutes)

**What**: Write down three good things that happened today and why they happened.
**Evidence**:
- "Three Good Things" practice: increased happiness and decreased depression that persisted and INCREASED at 3-month and 6-month follow-ups
- Weekly gratitude journaling for 10 weeks: more gratitude, positive moods, optimism, better sleep
- Meta-analysis (70 studies, 26,000+ people): higher gratitude → lower depression, higher life satisfaction, stronger social relationships
- People who kept gratitude journals exercised more and had fewer physical symptoms
**Protocol**: Write 3 good things before bed. Include why each happened. Can be done weekly or daily.
**Source**: Greater Good Science Center (Berkeley); PMC meta-analysis

## 9. SLEEP CONSISTENCY (7-9 hours, consistent timing)

**What**: Maintain consistent sleep/wake times; prioritize 7-9 hours.
**Evidence**:
- Sleep consistency (same bed/wake time) is MORE important than duration for health outcomes
- Irregular sleep timing linked to higher depression, anxiety, BMI, insulin resistance, hypertension, cardiovascular events
- Consistent sleep takes 2-4 weeks of practice to see improvements
- Screen reduction before bed improves sleep quality
**Key practices**:
- Consistent bed/wake time (±30 min, even weekends)
- Cool bedroom (65-68°F / 18-20°C)
- Dark room (blackout curtains or eye mask)
- No screens 30-60 min before bed
- No caffeine after noon
- No alcohol close to bedtime (disrupts REM)
**Source**: ScienceDirect (2025 systematic review); Sleep Foundation; APA

## 10. HYDRATION (throughout the day)

**What**: Drink water consistently throughout the day; start upon waking.
**Evidence**:
- Even mild dehydration (1-2% body water loss) impairs cognitive performance
- Dehydration → poor concentration, increased reaction time, short-term memory problems, moodiness, anxiety
- Water accounts for 75% of brain mass
- Rehydration rapidly restores cognitive function and mood
- Women show particular mood sensitivity to dehydration (vigor, alertness, fatigue, confusion)
**Protocol**: Water upon waking (before caffeine). Consistent intake throughout day. ~2-3 liters/day baseline, more with exercise/heat.
**Source**: British Journal of Nutrition; PMC; OSHA cognitive review

## 11. PROTEIN-FORWARD BREAKFAST (within 1-2 hours of waking)

**What**: First meal emphasizing protein over refined carbohydrates.
**Evidence**:
- High-protein breakfast suppresses postprandial glucose spikes after breakfast AND lunch ("second meal effect")
- Greater satiety, fewer cravings throughout the day
- Reduced blood sugar volatility → more stable mood and energy
- Early eating aligns with circadian metabolic rhythm (morning insulin sensitivity is highest)
**Protocol**: 25-40g protein in first meal. Eggs, Greek yogurt, meat, fish, legumes. Minimize refined carbs/sugar at breakfast.
**Source**: PMC (multiple RCTs); ScienceDirect (chrononutrition)

## 12. MICRO SOCIAL INTERACTIONS (throughout the day)

**What**: Brief, warm interactions with strangers, acquaintances, and colleagues — greeting, thanking, small talk.
**Evidence**:
- Commuters who engaged in minimal positive social interactions experienced greater life satisfaction and positive affect
- Connecting with strangers boosts mood — effects comparable in magnitude to close relationships
- Warm interaction with a barista → greater positive mood and sense of belonging
- Benefits are universal across Western and non-Western cultures
- "The emotional quality of interactions matters" — shared warmth, even briefly, accumulates into psychological benefits
**Source**: Journal of Happiness Studies; Gillian Sandstrom research; Psychology Today

## 13. DIGITAL BOUNDARIES (structured breaks)

**What**: Intentional screen-free periods — especially morning, meals, and before bed.
**Evidence**:
- 91% of participants in digital detox studies improved on at least one major well-being outcome
- Meta-analysis: digital detox significantly reduced depressive symptoms
- Phone-free meals → greater presence and emotional connection
- Morning phone-free period → reduced anxiety, more intentional day start
- Mixed research on total detoxes — partial/structured approaches more sustainable and still effective
**Protocol**: Phone-free first hour. Phone-free meals. No screens 30-60 min before bed. App timers for social media. Charge phone outside bedroom.
**Source**: PMC (multiple studies and meta-analysis); Georgetown University

## 14. SELF-COMPASSION MICRO-PRACTICE (20 seconds)

**What**: Hand on heart, opposite hand on belly. 20 seconds of self-compassionate touch.
**Evidence**: Already in wiki — 28+ days of 20-second daily practice significantly increased self-compassion and decreased stress, anxiety, and depression (Eli Susman, Behavior Research and Therapy, 2024).
**Source**: [GG 2024](../sources/greater-good-insights-2024.md)

## 15. WALKING (throughout the day)

**What**: Accumulate steps through the day — dedicated walks + incidental movement.
**Evidence**:
- 10,000 steps/day: significantly lower anxiety, depression, anger, fatigue, confusion
- 9,800 steps optimal for dementia risk reduction (50% lower)
- Even 2,500 steps significantly reduces all-cause mortality
- Walking increases blood flow to brain → cognitive function, mental clarity, focus
- Walking stimulates endorphins and serotonin
**Minimum**: 4,000-5,000 steps for beginners. Add 500-1,000/week.
**Source**: PMC (multiple studies); UCLA Health; Continental Hospitals review
