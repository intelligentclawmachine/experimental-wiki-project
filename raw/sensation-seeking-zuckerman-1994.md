# Behavioral Expressions and Biosocial Bases of Sensation Seeking — Zuckerman (1994)

## Key Findings

Sensation seeking defined as "the seeking of varied, novel, complex, and intense sensations and experiences, and the willingness to take physical, social, legal, and financial risks for the sake of such experience."

### Four Dimensions (SSS-V)
1. **Thrill and Adventure Seeking (TAS)** — desire for physical activities involving speed, danger, gravity
2. **Experience Seeking (ES)** — pursuit of novel sensory/mental experiences through unconventional lifestyle, travel, art, psychoactive substances
3. **Disinhibition (Dis)** — sensation through social stimulation, parties, sexual variety, alcohol
4. **Boredom Susceptibility (BS)** — aversion to repetition, routine, predictable environments

### Biological Basis
- **Optimal arousal theory** (building on Hebb 1955): high sensation seekers have lower baseline cortical arousal or higher optimal set-point
- **MAO-B**: low platelet MAO-B activity correlates with high sensation seeking (Zuckerman et al., 1980). MAO-B degrades dopamine and phenylethylamine; low MAO-B = more available catecholamines
- **Dopaminergic system**: greater dopamine release in mesolimbic pathway in response to novelty. DRD4 polymorphisms linked to sensation seeking (Roberti, 2004)
- **Cortical evoked potentials**: high sensation seekers show augmenting responses to increasing stimulus intensity; low seekers show reducing responses

### Connection to Risk and Substance Use
- One of the strongest personality predictors of substance use initiation, especially in adolescence (Hittner & Swickert, 2006 meta-analysis)
- Dis and BS subscales most strongly associated with alcohol and drug use
- Links to risky sexual behavior, reckless driving, gambling, criminal behavior
- Interacts with impulsivity (correlated but distinct — Whiteside & Lynam UPPS model, 2001)

### Healthy vs. Maladaptive Distinction
- Healthy: voluntary engagement, adequate risk assessment, skill development, post-experience integration
- Maladaptive: escalation (tolerance), loss of control, negative consequences fail to deter, stimulation used primarily to flee discomfort rather than approach reward
- Maps onto approach-motivated vs. avoidance-motivated sensation seeking
