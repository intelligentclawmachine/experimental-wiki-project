# How to Stop Attachment Insecurity from Ruining Your Love Life

**Source:** Laslocky, M. Greater Good Science Center, UC Berkeley.
**URL:** https://greatergood.berkeley.edu/article/item/how_to_stop_attachment_insecurity_from_ruining_your_love_life

## Overview

A research-backed article providing practical strategies for moving from insecure (including avoidant) attachment toward earned security in adult relationships.

## Two Types of Avoidant Attachment

### Dismissive-Avoidant
- High avoidance, low anxiety
- Strong preference for independence and self-reliance
- Uncomfortable with closeness and emotional intimacy
- May not recognize own attachment needs
- Uses deactivating strategies (emotional distancing, suppression)

### Fearful-Avoidant (Disorganized)
- High avoidance AND high anxiety
- Push-pull dynamics: wants closeness but fears it
- Oscillates between approach and withdrawal
- Often rooted in early trauma or inconsistent caregiving
- Most challenging pattern to change

## Five Evidence-Based Strategies for Earned Security

1. **Education about your pattern** — learning attachment theory helps recognize automatic reactions as patterns rather than "just who I am"
2. **Therapy with attachment-specialized clinicians** — therapists trained in attachment can work with the underlying model rather than just symptoms
3. **Seek relationships with securely attached partners** — approximately 50% of adults are securely attached; secure partners can serve as a "corrective emotional experience"
4. **Couples therapy** — even in healthy relationships, understanding attachment dynamics improves communication and reduces conflict
5. **Deliberate practice of uncomfortable vulnerability** — gradually increasing emotional exposure in safe contexts

## Security Priming Research

A key finding: security enhancement — whether long-term or even temporarily activated through priming — increases:
- Generosity toward others
- Compassion and empathy
- Tolerance of out-group members
- Willingness to help strangers

This suggests attachment security doesn't just help the individual — it radiates outward to improve social behavior and relationships broadly.

## The ~50% Secure Base Rate

Approximately 50% of the adult population is securely attached, meaning:
- Secure partners are available and common
- Relationship with a secure partner is one of the strongest pathways to earned security
- The secure partner provides a "safe base" that allows the insecure partner to gradually explore vulnerability

## Key Takeaway

Attachment patterns are not fixed traits but learnable skills. Earned security — developed through therapy, secure relationships, and deliberate practice — is associated with the same positive outcomes as naturally occurring security.
