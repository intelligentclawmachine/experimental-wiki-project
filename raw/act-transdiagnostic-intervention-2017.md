# Acceptance and Commitment Therapy as a Transdiagnostic Intervention

**Source:** Dindo, Van Liew, & Arch (2017). "Acceptance and Commitment Therapy: A Transdiagnostic Behavioral Intervention for Mental Health and Medical Conditions." PMC/NIH.
**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5509623/

## Overview

ACT is the primary clinical answer to experiential avoidance. It cultivates psychological flexibility — "the ability to persist in or change behavior when it is in service of valued ends" — through six core processes.

## The Hexaflex: Six Core Processes

1. **Present moment awareness** — contacting the present rather than ruminating on the past or worrying about the future
2. **Cognitive defusion** — learning to observe thoughts as mental events rather than literal truths ("I notice I'm having the thought that...")
3. **Acceptance** — willingness to experience difficult feelings without trying to eliminate them
4. **Self-as-context** — experiencing a stable sense of self that observes thoughts and feelings without being defined by them
5. **Values clarification** — identifying what truly matters and choosing directions based on values rather than avoidance
6. **Committed action** — taking concrete steps aligned with values, even in the presence of discomfort

## Psychological Flexibility as Inverse of Avoidance

ACT explicitly frames psychological flexibility as the opposite of experiential avoidance:
- Avoidance = narrowing behavior to escape discomfort
- Flexibility = expanding behavior in service of values despite discomfort

## Evidence Base

As of 2016:
- 136 published randomized controlled trials (RCTs)
- Medium effect sizes vs. treatment-as-usual
- Large effect sizes vs. wait-list controls
- APA recognizes ACT as empirically supported for: depression, mixed anxiety, psychosis, chronic pain, and OCD

## Transdiagnostic Application

ACT targets the underlying process (experiential avoidance) rather than specific diagnoses, making it applicable across:
- Mental health conditions (anxiety, depression, PTSD, eating disorders)
- Chronic medical conditions (chronic pain, diabetes management, cancer adjustment)
- Behavioral health (substance abuse, smoking cessation)
- Performance contexts (workplace stress, athletic performance)

## Key Distinction from CBT

While CBT focuses on changing the content of thoughts (cognitive restructuring), ACT focuses on changing the relationship to thoughts (defusion). Both approaches have strong evidence; they work through different mechanisms.
