# Post-Traumatic Growth

**Source:** Tedeschi (2023). "The Post-Traumatic Growth Approach." PMC/NIH.
**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10168173/

## Overview

Richard Tedeschi, who coined the term "post-traumatic growth" (PTG) in the mid-1980s, describes the theoretical model and clinical applications of growth following adversity.

## Core Mechanism

Trauma shatters fundamental assumptions about:
- Identity ("who I am")
- Life path ("where I'm going")
- Morality ("the world is just")
- Vulnerability ("bad things happen to other people")
- Predictability ("I can control my life")

This creates cognitive disorientation — a painful but necessary state that can lead to either avoidance (experiential avoidance, PTSD) or growth.

## The Growth Pathway

1. **Trauma/crisis** — fundamental assumptions are shattered
2. **Intrusive rumination** — automatic, distressing replay of events (this is NOT pathological — it's the mind trying to process)
3. **Transition to deliberate rumination** — the person begins actively reflecting on their changed beliefs and life narrative
4. **Reconstruction** — new, more nuanced schemas emerge that accommodate the trauma while incorporating growth
5. **Post-traumatic growth** — measurable positive changes across five domains

## Five Domains of Post-Traumatic Growth (PTGI)

1. **Improved relationships** — deeper intimacy, greater empathy, increased compassion
2. **New possibilities** — new paths, interests, or directions that wouldn't have been considered before
3. **Greater appreciation of life** — heightened gratitude, savoring of everyday moments
4. **Enhanced personal strength** — "I am stronger than I thought" — increased self-efficacy and resilience
5. **Spiritual/existential development** — deeper engagement with questions of meaning, purpose, transcendence

## Intrusive vs. Deliberate Rumination

A critical distinction:
- **Intrusive rumination** — automatic, unwanted, distressing (common in early aftermath)
- **Deliberate rumination** — intentional reflection on beliefs, meaning, and narrative (associated with growth)

The therapeutic goal is NOT to stop rumination but to facilitate the transition from intrusive to deliberate.

## Expert Companionship Model

Tedeschi's clinical approach uses "expert companionship" rather than traditional treatment:
1. **Trauma education** — normalizing responses, explaining the growth model
2. **Emotional regulation** — building capacity to tolerate distress
3. **Narrative disclosure** — telling the story in a safe environment
4. **Life narrative development** — integrating trauma into a coherent life story
5. **Service-oriented direction** — channeling growth into helping others

Programs use strength-affirming language ("students" not "patients") and show PTSD symptom reduction alongside growth.

## Key Insight

Growth is a complement to, not replacement for, symptom treatment. People can experience significant post-traumatic growth while still having PTSD symptoms. Growth and distress coexist — growth doesn't mean the trauma is "over" or "resolved."

## Contrast with Avoidance

When people face trauma:
- **Avoidance** prevents processing → maintains distress → blocks growth
- **Deliberate engagement** enables processing → reduces distress over time → enables growth
- PTG is the positive counterpart to experiential avoidance — what happens when people approach rather than avoid
