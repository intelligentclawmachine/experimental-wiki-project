# Experiential Avoidance Process Model

**Source:** Wang, Tian, & Yang (2024). "A Process Model of Experiential Avoidance." PMC/NIH.
**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11332439/

## Overview

This 2024 review proposes an integrated process model explaining how experiential avoidance — the unwillingness to engage with uncomfortable thoughts, emotions, sensations, and memories — is generated and maintained.

## Five-Stage Cascade Model

Building on Gross's emotion regulation framework and Linehan's emotional subsystems model, the authors trace a five-stage cascade:

1. **Emotional trigger** — an internal or external event activates negative affect
2. **Strategy selection** — the individual selects suppression/avoidance over approach
3. **Short-term relief** — avoidance temporarily reduces distress (negative reinforcement)
4. **Habitual consolidation** — repeated short-term relief creates automatic avoidance patterns
5. **Maladaptive entrenchment** — avoidance becomes the default response, narrowing behavioral repertoire

## The Downward Spiral

Avoidance provides temporary relief but creates a "downward spiral" through multiple cognitive mechanisms:

- **Attentional bias toward threats** — hypervigilance increases as avoidance prevents habituation
- **Working memory impairment** — suppression consumes cognitive resources
- **Memory bias** — negative memories are paradoxically strengthened by suppression attempts
- **Intensified emotional responses** — avoided emotions rebound with greater intensity (ironic process theory)

## Relational Frame Theory Connection

Hayes' Relational Frame Theory explains why humans are uniquely prone to experiential avoidance: language creates bidirectional links between neutral events and negative experiences. Thinking about a feared situation activates the same emotional response as the situation itself, making even thoughts targets for avoidance.

## Clinical Links

Strong empirical evidence connects experiential avoidance to:
- Depression (rumination as cognitive avoidance)
- Anxiety disorders (behavioral avoidance maintaining fear)
- OCD (thought suppression paradox — trying not to think about something increases intrusive thoughts)
- PTSD (emotional numbing and avoidance as core symptoms)

## Intervention Points

The model identifies intervention opportunities at each stage of the cascade, suggesting that early-stage interventions (changing strategy selection) are more effective than late-stage interventions (trying to undo entrenched patterns).
