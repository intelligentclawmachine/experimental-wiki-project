# Philosophy of Trust

**Source:** McLeod, Carolyn (2025). "Trust." Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.
**URL:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/trust/

Also informed by: Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on "The Ethics and Epistemology of Trust."

## Overview

The authoritative philosophical survey of trust theory, covering competing accounts of what trust is, what makes someone trustworthy, and why trust matters morally.

## Trust vs. Mere Reliance

The foundational philosophical question: what distinguishes trusting someone from merely relying on them?

- You can **rely** on an alarm clock, but you don't **trust** it
- The key difference: trust involves vulnerability to **betrayal**, not just **disappointment**
- When trust is violated, we feel betrayed — a specifically moral emotion that doesn't apply to unreliable objects
- Annette Baier: trust requires the trustee's **goodwill** toward the truster

## Major Competing Theories

### Baier's Goodwill Account
Trust requires believing the trusted person acts from benevolent motives. Trustworthiness = having goodwill toward the truster. Problem: this may be too demanding — we trust professionals (doctors, pilots) without requiring personal benevolence.

### Hardin's Encapsulated Interests
Trust = believing the trusted person has encapsulated your interests into their own. I trust you because I believe it's in YOUR interest to fulfill MY trust. Grounded in rational self-interest and reputation.

### Holton's Participant Stance
Trust involves adopting a "participant stance" — treating the trusted person as a responsible agent who can be held accountable. Trust is an attitude, not just a belief.

### Jones's Trust-Responsiveness
We trust people we expect to be "trust-responsive" — people who are motivated by the fact that we're counting on them. The trust itself creates a reason for the trustee to be trustworthy.

### Hawley's Commitment Account
Trust = relying on someone to fulfill a commitment they've made. Distrust = doubt that they will. This account elegantly explains why breaking promises feels like betrayal but incompetence doesn't.

### O'Neill's Trustworthiness Focus
Onora O'Neill argues we should not aim to *increase trust* but to *place trust well* and *refuse trust well*. The goal is not more trust but better judgment about when trust is warranted.

## Therapeutic Trust

A philosophically important concept: trusting someone *in order to make them trustworthy*. Parents do this with children; therapists with clients. Trust here is not based on evidence of trustworthiness but is an investment in creating it. This challenges purely evidence-based accounts of trust.

## Trust and Monitoring

Does monitoring undermine trust? Philosophical debate:
- Monitoring to reduce **betrayal risk** may signal distrust
- Monitoring to reduce **harm consequences** is compatible with trust
- The distinction matters for relationships, organizations, and institutions

## Trust and Testimonial Justice

Miranda Fricker's concept: **testimonial injustice** occurs when hearers wrongfully distrust speakers based on prejudice (race, gender, class). This connects trust to social justice — systematic distrust of certain groups is both epistemically and morally harmful.

## Key Takeaway

Trust is not a simple binary. It involves vulnerability to betrayal (not just disappointment), requires some form of goodwill or commitment from the trusted party, and serves as both a social lubricant and a moral relationship. The philosophical literature enriches the psychological research by clarifying what we mean when we say we "trust" someone.
