# 2026 Edelman Trust Barometer

**Source:** Edelman (2026). "2026 Edelman Trust Barometer."
**URL:** https://www.edelman.com/trust/2026/trust-barometer

## Overview

The 26th annual global survey of institutional trust, based on 30-minute interviews conducted October-November 2025 across multiple countries.

## Key Findings

### The Insularity Shift

The dominant trend: a shift from "grievance" to "insularity."
- **70% of respondents** are unwilling or hesitant to trust someone with different values, information sources, or cultural background
- People are retreating into smaller ideological circles
- Trust is becoming tribal — high within in-groups, low toward out-groups

### Generational Pessimism

- Only **32% globally** believe the next generation will be better off
- This represents a crisis of institutional trust — people don't trust systems to deliver future prosperity

### The Income Trust Gap

- The gap between high-income and low-income trust levels has **more than doubled since 2012**
- In the U.S.: a 29-point trust gap between wealthy and poor
- Inequality doesn't just create material disparity — it creates a trust disparity

### Institutional Trust Hierarchy

- **Business** remains the only institution seen as both ethical and competent
- Trust in government leaders dropped 16 points over five years
- NGOs and media occupy middle ground
- The pattern: institutions that deliver tangible value retain trust; those perceived as self-serving lose it

## Connection to Individual Psychology

The Edelman data connects to individual trust psychology:
- The in-group trust finding (70% insularity) maps to the meta-analysis finding that in-group membership has the largest effect on trust (r = 0.57)
- Inequality eroding baseline trust aligns with the contextual factors in trust formation
- The retreat into small circles mirrors Dunbar's layers — under threat, people shrink to their inner circles
- Institutional distrust may drive compensatory reliance on personal relationships (connecting to the Harvard Study finding that relationships matter most)
