# Attachment Theory in Adult Relationships

Source: R. Chris Fraley, University of Illinois
URL: https://labs.psychology.illinois.edu/~rcfraley/attachment.htm

## Origins: Bowlby's Theory

John Bowlby (1907-1990) developed attachment theory to explain infant distress during parental separation. The "attachment behavioral system" regulates proximity to attachment figures by continuously asking: "Is the attachment figure nearby, accessible, and attentive?"

- If YES → child feels loved, secure, confident; explores environment, is sociable and playful
- If NO → child experiences anxiety, engages in attachment behaviors (visual searching, active signaling/crying, moving toward figure)

## Infant Attachment Patterns (Ainsworth's Strange Situation)

Mary Ainsworth identified three patterns:
- **Secure** (~60%): upset at separation, easily comforted upon reunion
- **Anxious-Resistant** (~20%): extremely distressed, difficult to soothe, conflicted behavior
- **Avoidant** (~20%): minimal distress, actively avoid contact after reunion

Patterns correlated with parental responsiveness and sensitivity.

## Extension to Adult Romantic Relationships

Hazan and Shaver (1987) proposed romantic bonds operate through the same attachment system as infant-caregiver bonds. Shared features: seeking safety through proximity, intimate contact, mutual fascination, distress at separation.

## Two-Dimensional Model (Brennan)

Modern attachment measured on two continuous dimensions:
- **Attachment-related anxiety**: worry about partner availability and responsiveness (high = preoccupied)
- **Attachment-related avoidance**: preference for independence vs. intimacy (high = dismissive)

Four resulting styles:
1. **Secure** (low anxiety, low avoidance): comfortable with intimacy, confident in being valued
2. **Anxious-Preoccupied** (high anxiety, low avoidance): craves closeness, fears rejection
3. **Dismissive-Avoidant** (low anxiety, high avoidance): self-reliant, uncomfortable with dependence
4. **Fearful-Avoidant** (high anxiety, high avoidance): desires closeness but fears it

## Key Research Findings

- Secure adults show greater relationship satisfaction, longevity, trust, commitment, and interdependence
- Secure adults more likely to seek AND provide support during distress
- Partner selection mirrors infant patterns: adults prefer responsive, warm, attentive partners
- Consistent experiences with responsive partners can enhance attachment security over time (earned security)

## Stability Across Lifespan

- Parental-romantic attachment correlation: ~.20 to .50
- Infant security at age 1 → adult romantic security 20 years later: r = .17
- Attachment influences continue throughout life regardless of individual stability

## Important Nuance

Attachment is dimensional, not categorical. People fall on a spectrum. And attachment security can be earned in adulthood through positive relationship experiences — it's not fixed by childhood.
