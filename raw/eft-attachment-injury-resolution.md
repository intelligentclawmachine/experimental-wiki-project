# Attachment Injury Resolution Model (AIRM) in Emotionally Focused Therapy

**Source:** Johnson (2001), Brubacher (2018). Attachment Injury Resolution Model in EFT.
**URLs:** https://trieft.org/wp-content/uploads/2012/01/Attachment-Injuries.pdf | https://cls.unc.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/3019/2018/12/Forgiveness-Trust_AIRM-in-EFT.Brubacher2018.pdf

## Overview

The Attachment Injury Resolution Model is the specific protocol within Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT) for trust repair after betrayal. Developed by Sue Johnson and refined by Lorrie Brubacher, it treats betrayal as an "attachment injury" — an abandonment or violation of trust during a critical moment of need that redefines the relationship as insecure.

## What Is an Attachment Injury?

Not just a hurt — an attachment injury becomes the *ongoing standard* by which the injured partner judges the offending partner's reliability. It is a specific incident where one partner failed to respond when the other desperately needed them, shattering the sense of the relationship as a safe haven.

Examples: infidelity discovery, abandonment during illness, failure to protect during a crisis, emotional unavailability during grief.

## EFT as Evidence-Based Treatment

- EFT is the only evidence-based treatment for couples in distress recognized by the APA
- 70-75% success rate
- Lasting increases in relationship satisfaction at 2-year follow-up
- Works by accessing "soft" emotions (fear, grief, longing) underneath "hard" emotions (anger, withdrawal)

## The Three-Stage Model

### Stage 1: De-escalation
- Identify the negative interaction cycle (pursue-withdraw, attack-defend)
- Help each partner see the cycle as the enemy, not the other person
- Reduce emotional reactivity so the deeper work can begin

### Stage 2: Restructuring Interactions
- The injured partner expresses deep hurt — not anger but the vulnerable emotions underneath (fear of being unlovable, grief over lost safety)
- The injuring partner moves into genuine accountability and empathic engagement — not just cognitive acknowledgment but emotional presence
- The injuring partner becomes accessible and responsive in the moment of the injured partner's vulnerability
- This is the "change event" — the moment when the attachment injury begins to heal

### Stage 3: Consolidation
- New patterns of comfort and caring become self-reinforcing
- The couple develops a new narrative about the injury and their recovery
- The relationship is redefined as a secure base again — but with the scar tissue of hard-won wisdom

## Key Insight

AIRM doesn't aim to "fix" the betrayal or pretend it didn't happen. It restructures the emotional bond so that the couple develops a new, more resilient form of secure attachment — one that has been tested by adversity and found strong enough to hold.

## Connection to Attachment Theory

EFT is explicitly grounded in attachment theory:
- Betrayal = attachment injury = the safe haven is no longer safe
- Repair = re-establishing the partner as a secure base
- The injured partner's anger is a protest behavior (like an infant's cry) — a bid for the lost connection
- The injuring partner's withdrawal is a deactivating strategy — avoidance of the shame and pain
